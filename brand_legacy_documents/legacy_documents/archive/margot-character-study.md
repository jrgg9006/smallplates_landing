# Brand Character Profile
## Small Plates & Co. — Margot Cole
### Version 2.0

---

# Introduction

This document is not a brand personality guide. It's deeper than that.

This is the complete profile of **Margot Cole** — the woman who *is* Small Plates. She is not a customer persona. She is not a target demographic. She is not the bride, the MOH, or the mom.

**She is the brand.**

When the bride discovers Small Plates, she sees Margot and thinks: *this brand gets me.*
When the MOH discovers Small Plates, she sees Margot and thinks: *I want to be the person who gives this.*
When the mom discovers Small Plates, she sees Margot and thinks: *this is exactly what my daughter would love — and honestly, so do I.*

One person. Every audience finds herself in her.

When you need to make a decision about Small Plates — what to say, how to say it, what to design, what to reject — ask yourself:

> **"What would Margot do?"**

If she would love it: do it.
If she would ignore it: improve it.
If it would make her cringe: kill it.

She is your filter. Your standard. Your compass.

# Section 1: Who She Is

## The Basics

**Name:** Margot Cole

**Age:** 28 years old

**Where she lives:** Brooklyn, New York. Specifically Cobble Hill — not Williamsburg (too obvious, too 2015), not Manhattan (she's past that). Cobble Hill is residential but alive, has beautiful brownstones, is close to good food, and feels like a real neighborhood, not a set.

**Her apartment:** A one-bedroom on the third floor of a brownstone. Incredible natural light. Small but real kitchen — she actually uses it. A table for four that fits six when needed. Plants that don't die. Art she bought on a trip or from a friend. A bookshelf with books she's actually read, some with receipts still inside as bookmarks. Nothing screams "look at my great taste" — she simply has it.

**What she does:** Creative Director at an independent creative studio. Small, sharp, does brand work for companies that are trying to say something real. She's not famous, not an influencer, doesn't need anyone to know who she is. But in her world — brands, culture, visual identity — she's the person people call when they want something to actually work.

**Why this job matters:** She left a bigger agency three years ago because she was tired of making things look good that had nothing to say. She wanted to build things that had a point of view. Now she does. It's harder and pays less but she doesn't lose sleep over it.

**Salary:** Comfortable but not rich. Can travel once or twice a year. Can afford the good dinner. Doesn't buy luxury for status, but invests in things worth it.

**Relationship status:** Has been with her partner for three years. They live together. They're probably getting married at some point — neither of them is in a rush, and both of them like it that way.

# Section 2: Where She Comes From

## Her Origins

**Where she grew up:** Connecticut. A nice suburb of New Haven — not the Connecticut of Greenwich hedge fund money, but comfortable. House with a yard. Good public schools. The kind of place you're glad you grew up in and glad you left.

**Her family:**

**Dad:** History professor. Academic but not pretentious. Taught her to ask questions, to sit with ambiguity, to not accept easy answers. They had dinner together most nights growing up and he always asked "what did you learn today?" — not to evaluate, genuinely curious.

**Mom:** Worked in nonprofit arts administration. The kind of woman who could make a dinner party feel like an event using only what was in the fridge and some candles. She taught Margot that beauty is about attention, not budget.

**One younger brother:** Works in tech in San Francisco. They're close in the way of siblings who are different but fundamentally the same. He's more optimistic than she is. She finds it endearing.

**How her upbringing shaped her:**

She grew up with enough — not more. That gave her standards without entitlement. She knows the difference between quality and price. She knows that a dinner with the right people beats a fancy restaurant with the wrong ones. She's not sentimental about things, but she is sentimental about people. She doesn't hold on to objects. She holds on to relationships.

# Section 3: Her Point of View on Weddings

*This section is new and critical. It defines how Margot — and therefore Small Plates — stands in relationship to the wedding industry.*

---

## Margot Has Been to Eleven Weddings in Four Years

She loved every single one of them, for different reasons. She also found most of them, at some point, a little too much.

She's sat through the eight-minute best man speech. She's watched $600 centerpieces get thrown in a dumpster the next morning. She's received the save-the-date for a wedding in Tuscany for 200 people that cost the couple three years of savings and lasted one day.

She doesn't judge any of it. She just notices.

**What she thinks about weddings, honestly:**

The wedding industry sells a fantasy to people who are genuinely in love. Sometimes that fantasy is exactly what they need. And sometimes it gets in the way of the actual thing — the people, the meaning, the moment.

She believes most wedding products are designed for the *day*. Small Plates is designed for *everything after*.

She believes most wedding gifts are purchased on autopilot — people going through a registry like a checkout line, picking something that checks a box. She's done it herself. She's not proud of it.

She believes the best wedding she's ever attended was her college friend's. Forty people. A barn in Vermont. The bride wore her grandmother's dress. Someone's dad played guitar. Nobody checked their phone. She cried twice and didn't see it coming either time.

**What she believes about the wedding industry:**

It's built for millennials who wanted the perfect day. Gen Z wants the real one.

She's the first brand to understand the difference.

## How This POV Shapes Small Plates

Margot doesn't take weddings too seriously. That's what makes her trustworthy to the people who take weddings very seriously.

She can find the rehearsal dinner mildly absurd and cry during the vows. She can think the $200 floral arrangement is excessive and be deeply moved by the handwritten recipe her aunt submitted.

**The bride who discovers Small Plates via Margot:**
Feels seen. Thinks: *finally, a wedding brand that doesn't make me feel like I need to perform.*

**The MOH who discovers Small Plates via Margot:**
Feels inspired. Thinks: *this is the thing I organize that nobody else will think to do. This is my moment.*

**The mom who discovers Small Plates via Margot:**
Feels permission. Thinks: *I was going to get something safe. But this is actually what I want to give her.*

# Section 4: Her Taste and Aesthetic

## What She Actually Likes

**In visual design:** Clean but not cold. Warm but not soft. She's drawn to things with restraint — where a choice was made to leave something out and it made the whole thing better. She notices typography the way other people notice shoes.

**In photography:** Real over beautiful. She prefers a slightly blurry photo of something honest over a perfectly lit photo of something arranged. She follows photographers who shoot kitchens that look lived in, tables with food already half-eaten, hands doing things rather than posing.

**In fashion:** She has a uniform. She's figured it out and she's not changing it. Good quality basics. A few investment pieces. Something unexpected that most people miss on first look — a vintage belt, a very specific shade of brown she found in Tokyo. She's not trying to be interesting. She is.

**In food:** She cooks. Actually cooks, not performatively cooks. She has a cast iron she's had since she was 23 that she's never gotten rid of. She has recipes she knows by heart because she's made them so many times. She's moved past following recipes exactly — she knows when to trust herself.

**In music:** Wide taste. Doesn't perform it. Has a playlist for cooking that's been evolving for four years.

## What She Doesn't Like

Generic quality. Things made for the photo. Brands that explain their personality instead of having one. Weddings that feel like a production the couple is performing in rather than experiencing. Gifts that say "I didn't know what to get you."

She never says any of this out loud. She just quietly moves toward the things that are real and away from the things that aren't.

# Section 5: Her Relationship with Social Media

## How She Actually Uses It

Margot has a presence on Instagram. Not large, but real. Around 3,000 followers who are mostly people she's actually met plus some who found her through her work. She posts when she has something worth posting — never for the sake of it.

She doesn't do TikTok herself, but she watches it. Late at night, more than she admits. She follows people who are saying real things in a direct way. She clicks away from anything trying too hard. She has strong opinions about what makes a good video: *the hook has to be true. Everything else can be craft.*

## What She Notices on Social

She's extremely good at detecting when something is performing versus being. She can tell in under three seconds if a brand is trying to speak to her or just speaking at her.

**What she saves:**
Things that say something she's thought but never put into words. Images where she wants to live. Lines of copy she wishes she'd written.

**What she scrolls past:**
Anything aspirational without specificity. Beautiful things with nothing behind them. Brands that think warmth is a visual filter.

**What she shares:**
Rarely. But when she does, it means she actually means it. And the people who receive it know that.

## Margot and TikTok as a Brand Channel

Margot doesn't create TikTok content. **But Small Plates' TikTok content sounds like she would.**

That's the test. Every video, every hook, every line: would Margot share this? Would she feel represented by it?

If yes: it goes out.
If it's trying too hard to be Gen Z: she'd find it embarrassing.
If it's too editorial and serious: she'd find it out of touch.

The sweet spot is where she lives: **direct, a little dry, emotionally honest. Never performative. Never precious.**

# Section 6: Her Relationship to Weddings as a Guest

## When She's a Guest

She shows up. She dresses well without overdressing. She doesn't spend the night on her phone. She dances if the music is good. She's genuinely happy for the couple.

She also notices everything. The details that were thought through. The moments that weren't planned. The speech that actually said something. The one that went four minutes too long.

**When she picks a gift:**
Never from the registry unless she genuinely loves something on it. She thinks about the specific couple. What will they use? What will make them think of her in a year? What would she want to receive?

**When she discovers Small Plates:**
She stops. She reads everything. She sends it to three people immediately. Not as a question — as a statement. *"This is what I'm getting them."*

## When She's the MOH

She's been the MOH twice. She takes it seriously the way she takes everything seriously — quietly, completely.

She doesn't make it about her. She makes it about making the bride feel seen. She's good at logistics. She doesn't need credit in the moment. She gets it eventually, which is enough.

She finds most MOH gift ideas on Pinterest slightly embarrassing. She's always looking for something that feels like *her* — elevated, thoughtful, not available at Target.

**Small Plates, to Margot as MOH, is the first thing she's found that makes her want to say:** *"Yes. This is me. This is what I would organize."*

# Section 7: Her Contradictions

Margot isn't perfect. Her contradictions are what make her human — and what make the brand human through her.

| What she says / believes | The reality |
|--------------------------|-------------|
| "I don't care what others think" | Sometimes she does. More than she'd admit. |
| "Balance is important" | There are weeks she works too much and won't acknowledge it. |
| "I don't judge" | She does judge. She tries not to, but she has strong opinions about most things. |
| "Social media doesn't affect me" | Sometimes she compares herself. Sometimes she feels less. |
| "I'm fine with things being imperfect" | She redoes things until they're right and calls it "iteration." |
| "I don't like drama" | She loves a good story about drama. She calls it "staying informed." |
| "I'm not materialistic" | Aesthetics matter to her enormously. She just doesn't call it materialism. |

**What this means for the brand:**

Small Plates doesn't need to be perfect either. It can acknowledge the tension between the beautiful idea and the messy reality of making it happen. That honesty is what earns trust.

# Section 8: How She Speaks

## Her Voice

Margot speaks like she thinks: clear, specific, without excess. She doesn't use ten words when five will do. She doesn't exaggerate to make a point. She's warm but not effusive. Funny but not performing it. Direct but not cold.

**The test:** Would you read her message and feel like she was the smartest person in the room who also happened to care about you? Then it's right.

## Her Register Has Shifted Slightly

The Margot of Version 1 was precise and editorial. That's still true.

The Margot of Version 2 has a little more looseness. She can be dry. She can be slightly wry about the wedding industry. She can say "okay but this is kind of ridiculous" and mean it affectionately. Her emotional intelligence is intact. Her willingness to be irreverent about the things that deserve it is new.

**She can laugh at the wedding industry and cry at the wedding. In that order. Always in that order.**

## Phrases She Uses

**When she likes something:**

| Situation | What she says |
|-----------|---------------|
| Something is really good | "This is *so* good." Real emphasis, not performative |
| Something surprised her | "Okay wait. This is actually — yeah." |
| An incredible meal | Closes her eyes for a second. Says nothing. That's the compliment. |
| Something is exactly her style | "Very me." |
| Recommending something | "Trust me on this one." Not "you should try this" — *trust me*. |

**When she doesn't like something:**

| Situation | What she says |
|-----------|---------------|
| Something is generic | "It's... fine." The pause does the work. |
| Something is too much | "It's a lot." |
| Something is trying too hard | "They're working really hard at this." |
| Something is overrated | "Honestly? Skip it." |
| Something is cheesy | A look. Nothing else needed. |

**When she talks about weddings:**

| Situation | What she says |
|-----------|---------------|
| A wedding was beautiful | "I ugly cried and I'm not apologizing for it." |
| A wedding was too much | "I love them and it was a *lot* of money for one day." |
| A generic wedding gift | "I did my best with the registry." (Code for: I phoned it in and I know it.) |
| Small Plates | "Why didn't this exist before." Not a question. |

## Words She Would Never Say

| Word / Phrase | Why |
|---------------|-----|
| "Cherish" | Too greeting card |
| "Treasure" | Same |
| "Journey" | She's going somewhere, not on a journey |
| "Amazing" | Overused to the point of meaning nothing |
| "Curated" | Unless she's actually curating something |
| "Perfect" | She knows nothing is |
| "So blessed" | Immediate loss of credibility |
| "I'm obsessed" (for everything) | Only when she really is |
| "Literally dying" (constantly) | Occasionally, never as a reflex |
| "Yummy" | She's an adult |
| "Memories" | She'd say *the people*, not the memory of them |

# Section 9: The Margot Tests

## The Classic Test — For Everything

> "Would our 28-year-old Brooklyn Creative Director, glass of natural wine in hand at a dinner party that got real at 11pm, say this?"

If she'd roll her eyes → rewrite.
If she'd share it without explanation → ship it.

## The New Test — For Gen Z Relevance

> "Would the MOH at a 40-person wedding in someone's backyard in Ojai, who found Small Plates at 2am on TikTok, feel like this brand was made for her — or made for her mom?"

If it feels like it was made for the mom → rewrite.
If it feels like the mom would discover it through her daughter and love it → ship it.

The second condition is the goal. The brand travels from daughter to mother, not the other way around.

## The Emotion Test — For Copy Specifically

Every piece of copy has to follow the sequence. No exceptions.

1. **"Ha"** — the smart observation. The thing everyone knows but nobody says out loud.
2. **"...oh"** — the universal truth underneath it.
3. **"...damn"** — the feeling you didn't see coming.

Skip to the emotion: greeting card.
Stay at the observation: Twitter.
The power is in the turn.

# Section 10: How Margot Sees Each Audience

*Margot is the brand. Each audience doesn't get a different Margot — they get the same Margot from a different angle.*

## The Bride Sees Margot As...

The brand that finally gets that she doesn't want her wedding to feel like every other wedding she's attended. Margot respects her intelligence. Doesn't tell her what to feel. Trusts that she'll feel it.

**What the bride thinks:** *This brand understands that I'm not just a bride. I'm a person who's getting married.*

## The MOH Sees Margot As...

The version of herself she wants to be in this moment. Organized, tasteful, the person in the group who found the thing nobody else found. Small Plates gives the MOH Margot's taste on loan — and that's exactly what she wants.

**What the MOH thinks:** *If I give this, everyone will know it was me who figured it out. And they'll be right.*

## The Mom Sees Margot As...

Trustworthy. Elevated. Not trying to sell her something — trying to say something. The mom doesn't need to fully understand Margot. She just needs to trust that her daughter would.

**What the mom thinks:** *I don't know if I would have chosen this myself, but I know she'll love it. And honestly — I love it too.*

## The Guest Sees Margot As...

Low pressure. A brand that respects their time and doesn't make them feel guilty for being busy. Margot doesn't guilt-trip. She makes things easy and then gets out of the way.

**What the guest thinks:** *This took me five minutes and it actually means something. I'd do this again.*

# Section 11: How Margot Relates to the Organizer (MOH)

## The Evolution

In Version 1, Margot related to the MOH as COO to COO — two competent women, professional handoff, no hand-holding.

That's still true. But there's a layer missing.

**Margot doesn't just *respect* the MOH. She *is* the MOH energy.**

She's been that friend. The one who coordinated the group trip nobody else could coordinate. Who sent the reminder texts without being asked. Who researched every option and came back with a recommendation, not a list.

She knows the invisible labor. The group chats that go nowhere. The "I'll definitely contribute!" that requires three follow-ups. The private satisfaction of watching something she built quietly become the best part of someone else's day.

## What the MOH Needs from Margot

Not encouragement. Not warmth. **Evidence of competence and a role she can be proud of.**

Small Plates gives the MOH a job that looks effortless from the outside and requires real effort to execute. That's exactly the kind of thing Margot would organize. That's exactly the kind of thing the MOH wants to be known for.

**The line the MOH needs to feel:**
*"I set this up. They handled the rest. And it was the best gift she got."*

## How This Shapes Brand Voice for the Organizer

| Situation | How Margot Would Handle It |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Onboarding the organizer | Here's exactly what happens. Here's exactly what you do. Done. |
| Progress update | "32 of 45 guests have submitted. Reminder going out tomorrow." Numbers. Not feelings. |
| Something goes wrong | Tell her directly. Propose the solution. Don't make her worry about the problem. |
| She needs to explain it to others | One line she can copy-paste: *"Everyone who's coming submits a recipe and a note. They turn it into a beautiful book."* |
| She's done | Acknowledge it privately, not performatively. She didn't do it for the applause. |

# Section 12: Brand Decisions Margot Makes

## What She Would Always Do

- Lead with the specific, not the general
- Trust the audience to feel without being told what to feel
- Leave more white space than feels comfortable
- Cut the last sentence (it's usually where the explanation hides)
- Choose the photograph where something is slightly off over the one that's perfect
- Say the thing that makes people slightly uncomfortable because it's true

## What She Would Never Do

- Use a stock photo of a couple looking at each other with their mouths open in joy
- Write a tagline that sounds like a tagline
- Explain why something is emotional instead of making it emotional
- Show the product in a context where it's the most important thing in the room
- Use more than two exclamation points in any piece of communication
- Design something that looks like it came from The Knot

## The Full Decision Filter

| Question | What Margot Requires |
|----------|---------------------|
| Would she use these words? | No generic marketing language. No wedding industry clichés. |
| Would she put this in her apartment? | Must fit a real home, not a styled set. |
| Would she send this to a friend? | No explanation required. It stands alone. |
| Would she trust this brand? | Honest, specific, doesn't oversell. |
| Does it follow Ha → Oh → Damn? | The emotion is earned, not announced. |
| Does it pass the MOH test? | Would a 26-year-old feel represented, not condescended to? |
| Does it pass the mom test? | Would she discover it through her daughter and trust it? |
