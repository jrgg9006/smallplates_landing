# Small Plates — Brand Idea

## THE IDEA

**People you love shouldn't disappear from your life.**

The forces of modern life — distance, time, technology, attention — work against this every day. We work for it.

---

## THE ENEMY

**The Forgettable.**

Most things made today are designed to be seen once and discarded. Gifts that go in a drawer. Posts that vanish in a swipe. Connection that performs itself and then disappears.

The more digital life gets, the more forgettable it gets.

---

## WHY WE EXIST

This generation has more contact than any generation in history and feels more alone. The internet promised belonging and delivered notifications. Presence has been inflated and devalued.

We rebuild it. Not with another app. With objects you hold, use, and stain — that put the people who matter back in the room with you.

---

## WHY NOW

After a decade of mass digitalization, a generation raised entirely online is actively choosing physical objects, analog rituals, and experiences that require presence. Not out of nostalgia — they have no nostalgia for it. Out of hunger.

The culture is arriving at something Small Plates was built around from the start.

---

## WHAT WE MAKE

A collaborative recipe book from the people who love someone — for a wedding, a retirement, a graduation, a new baby, a birthday that matters. The organizer starts it free. Everyone contributes a recipe and a note through a link, from wherever they are. We clean, design, print, and bind. Payment happens once, when the book goes to print. It lives in the kitchen.

The book is the proof. Presence is the product.

This is the first thing we make. It won't be the last.

---

## WHY PHYSICAL

The obvious alternative is digital — a shared album, a website, a video montage. Cheaper. Easier. More shareable.

But a physical object made by a whole group carries a signal no individual purchase can replicate: *this required coordination.* Someone organized it. Everyone contributed. The weight of it — literal and figurative — is the proof. You can hold it. You can open it in front of people and watch them look for their own name. It passes from hand to hand. It gets stained and stays on the counter. Digital things don't do this.

The value is not the recipes. The value is the artifact of everyone taking part — made holdable and permanent.

This is also why price is a feature, not a problem. A cheap book would undermine the signal. Starting is free; the book is not. The price says: these people were worth it.

## WHAT "SMALL PLATES & CO." MEANS

The "& Co." isn't a corporate suffix.

It means *the company of people.* Whoever's in your life that matters — still here, still at the table.

That's the whole idea.

## HOW TO EXPLAIN IT

*Five sentences. For press, investors, cold intros, anyone who asks.*

Small Plates & Co. makes collaborative recipe books — one hardcover, written by the people who love someone. The organizer starts it free and shares a link; people send a recipe and a note. ## WHAT WE PROMISE

We don't promise the past. We promise presence.

Photo albums archive people. We keep them active. Passive vs. active presence is the only category distinction that matters.

## HOW WE POSITION

The occasion industry — weddings, showers, retirements, all of it — runs on rules. We break five on purpose. Every piece of work should carry at least one of them legibly.

1. **We reject the occasion industry's verbal playbook.** They sell "the big day." We talk about the afterward — who stays, what gets used. The vocabulary rules live in `voice.md`.
2. **The buyer is the organizer, not the honoree.** Behaviorally defined, not demographically — the one who opens the group chat. Segmentation stops there.
3. **The gift is collective, not individual.** The registry model is one-guest-one-thing. We invert it: the whole group co-writes a single object.
4. **Value activates in the afterward.** Most occasion products consume their value on the day — flowers, catering, cake. Ours starts working after.
5. **The only gift that activates socially when opened.** People crowd around, look for their own recipe, pass the book hand to hand. That's structural, not a side effect. It's how the product spreads.

**We deviate deliberately on:** editorial typography (Minion Pro + Inter, never script — see `visual-identity.md`); photographing the book in use — stained, open, next to flour — never styled and pristine; real domestic contexts; the honoree is never the visual protagonist. The contributors are, through their handwriting and recipes.

**We respect deliberately:** the warm earth-tone palette (not ownable in this category — we differentiate in narrative, not color) and the rectangular bound book (no gimmick formats). Nobody should try to "disrupt" these.

**Watch out for:** "hyper-personalization" as language (a cliché every occasion brand uses — we sell presence, not personalization); "timeless" as aspiration (our framing is active, present tense); light-and-airy product photography (the category default — reject it).
