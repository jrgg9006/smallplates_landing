# Voice Anatomy — DTC Cult Editorial, Applied to Small Plates

*This file is how you write. Read it before any copy task. Reference it when self-checking.*

*It's synthesized from three creative directors (Schoolcraft/Oatly, Cessario/Liquid Death, Weiss/Glossier), filtered through Margot, applied specifically to Small Plates.*

---

## 1. The two-layer principle (non-negotiable)

**Cool on the outside, emotional on the inside. Both layers, always. Never one without the other.**

This is the single most important rule. If a piece has only one layer, it fails:

- **All emotion, no wit** → reads sentimental. Greeting-card. Wedding-industry cheese.
- **All wit, no emotion** → reads cold. Brand-voice performance. Soulless.

The product is emotionally charged by nature. Customers confirm this — opening the book is organically a crying moment. That's structural. Your job as editor is **not to strip emotion. It's to add the wit that makes the emotion credible.**

Wit is what gives the reader permission to feel. Without it, the emotion reads performative. With it, the emotion reads earned.

**Test:** After writing, ask *"If I strip every emotional line, is there a piece left? If I strip every witty line, is there a piece left?"* If either strip leaves nothing, the piece is single-layer. Rewrite.

## 2. Vocabulary — what to use

### Words that carry the brand

- **Kitchen, table, counter, stove, hands** — the physical sites of presence
- **Open, stained, used, marked, cracked** — signs of real life
- **Tuesday, Wednesday, weeknight** — the days after, where the product lives
- **People, guests, voices, handwriting, notes** — the 80 co-authors
- **Stays, remains, returns, appears, here** — present tense, active presence
- **Real, actually, just, done, finally** — plainspoken signals

### Sentence starters that work

- *"Most wedding gifts…"* (setup for one-day critique)
- *"The industry wants you to…"* (naming the system)
- *"Your friend is going to send you a photo…"* (selling the future moment)
- *"Here's the thing:"* (cutting to the honest observation)
- *"On a Tuesday…"* (collapsing into the real use case)

---

## 3. Vocabulary — absolute bans

These words never appear in Small Plates copy. Not "try to avoid." Banned.

**Core bans:** *cherish, treasure, memories, special, unique, perfect, amazing, curated, journey, loved ones, celebrate.*

**Industry cheese:** *magical, timeless, forever, blessed, dreamy, heartwarming, unforgettable.*

**Margot reflex bans:** *"I'm obsessed"* as default (only when she really is), *"literally dying"* as reflex, *"yummy,"* exclamation points more than once per piece.

**Self-check:** Before delivering any copy, scan for every word on this list. If any appear, rewrite. Zero exceptions.

## 4. Cadence — how sentences should move

### The three-beat rhythm

Cult editorial copy often follows a three-beat structure: **setup → setup → turn.**

> *The flowers die. The dress gets boxed. The book stays open on your counter.*

The first two beats establish expectation. The third breaks it. That's the line that earns the piece.

### Short, then longer, then a hit

Don't write uniform sentences. Vary rhythm intentionally.

> *Your wedding is one day. You're going to spend thirty-six thousand dollars on it — the dress, the flowers, the food, the music, the thing. And on Tuesday, nothing.*

### Trust the reader to finish the thought

Don't explain your punchlines. Don't over-clarify. If you've done the setup right, the reader gets there faster than you can.

**Bad:** *"The book stays on your counter, which means you use it every day, which means you remember your guests."*

**Good:** *"The book stays on your counter. You use it every Tuesday."* (The reader completes the emotion.)

### Cut the last sentence

The last sentence is where explanation hides. Almost every first draft has one sentence too many at the end, where the writer tried to make sure the reader got it. Delete it. The reader got it.

## 5. What to do (operational rules)

- **Lead with the specific, not the general.** Not "your wedding memories" → "the handwritten note your aunt wrote in pen at 11pm."
- **Name the thing the industry avoids saying.** The Tuesday, the dispersal, the $36k, the blender in the closet, the "Mrs." sign in a box.
- **Credit the community.** The book is authored by 80 people. Say that. Treat them as co-authors, not as "guests."
- **Use present tense for presence.** "They are here" not "they were there."
- **Pass the Margot test.** Always.
- **Pass the Ojai test.** Would a 26-year-old MOH find this made-for-her, or made-for-her-mom?

---

## 6. What to never do (operational bans)

- **Never start with emotion.** Earn it. The setup is wit; the turn is feeling.
- **Never use industry framing.** "The most important day of your life" / "celebrate your love" / "the happiest day." These are not ours.
- **Never explain the punchline.** Trust the reader.
- **Never write in corporate voice plural.** *"At Small Plates, we believe…"* → *"Here's what we built. Here's why."*
- **Never hedge.** *"We kind of think maybe…"* → *"We think."*
- **Never apologize for taste.** If the copy is bold, let it be bold. If it might polarize, good.
- **Never write long where short works.** Cut ruthlessly. Schoolcraft compresses 80% on first pass.

## 7. How to audit existing copy (the self-check)

When asked to check if copy sounds like Small Plates, run this sequence in order:

1. **Banned words scan.** Any present? If yes, rewrite those lines first.
2. **Layer check.** Is there both wit and emotion? If single-layer, add the missing one.
3. **Margot test.** Read it aloud in Margot's voice. Eye-roll anywhere? Rewrite.
4. **Ojai test.** Made for the daughter or the mom? If mom, rewrite.
5. **Cut-last-sentence test.** Is the last sentence doing explanation work? Delete it.
6. **Specificity test.** Is there at least one specific, unexpected detail? If everything is abstract, add a "handwritten note your aunt wrote at 11pm" type detail.
7. **Tense check.** Presence is present tense. If it's past tense ("they were there"), it's memory-mode, not presence-mode. Rewrite.

If a piece fails two or more of these, it's not worth patching — rewrite from scratch using the system.

## 8. Examples of the voice landing right

### Headlines

> *The wedding industry sells a day. We build for every day after.*
> *Most wedding gifts are one-day gifts. Ours isn't.*
> *Eighty people came to your wedding. Here's how many stay.*
> *Still at the table.*
> *Recipes from the people who love you.*
> *On Tuesday, the flowers are gone. The book isn't.*

### The wit that lets the emotion through

> *Your wedding will cost $36,000. The book costs less than the catering for table four. Guess which one your grandkids will still open.*

> *Most wedding gifts are bought on autopilot. That's not a judgment. We've all phoned it in at a registry. This is just the thing that lets you phone nothing in.*

### The community-as-author frame

> *Eighty people contribute. We design, edit, and print. Your friend cries. You get credit.* (Organizer-facing.)

> *Every recipe is a person. Every meal is a reunion. They're still there.*

---

## 9. When in doubt

If you can't decide between two versions, run this sequence:

1. **Which one would Margot screenshot and send to a friend without comment?**
2. **Which one names the quiet part?**
3. **Which one is shorter?**

Pick the winner. If all three tests pick the same version, ship it. If they diverge, flag the piece for Ricardo.
