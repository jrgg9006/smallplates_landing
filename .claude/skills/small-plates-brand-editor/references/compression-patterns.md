# Compression Patterns — Examples From Real Work

*This file teaches the Schoolcraft compression method by example, not by rule. Read the examples. Absorb the pattern. Apply.*

*Rule of thumb: aggressive brand doc compression produces ~80% reduction on first pass. If your compressed version is less than half the original, you haven't cut enough.*

---

## The compression philosophy (in one paragraph)

Brand docs written by agencies are essays. Brand docs written by cult brands are tools. An essay explains; a tool produces decisions. When compressing, your job is to move from essay to tool. Every sentence that doesn't produce a decision is a sentence to cut. Every sentence that justifies a decision the reader will already accept is a sentence to cut. Every sentence that repeats a point already made is a sentence to cut. What remains is the tool.

## Example 1 — The Margot character profile

**Original doc:** 4,500 words. A literary character study.

**Compressed doc:** ~500 words. An operational filter.

**Reduction:** 89%.

### What was cut

- Biography (Connecticut, her parents, boyfriend, 3,000 followers) → archived, not in operational doc
- Her "contradictions" section (literary, not operational) → archived
- Four different articulations of "she is the brand" → reduced to one
- Version 1 vs Version 2 discussion → removed (history, not tool)
- The "Ha → Oh → Damn" emotion test → removed (not being used)
- Audience-by-audience breakdown → migrated to messaging framework

### What was kept

- The opening premise (Margot is filter, not persona)
- Two operational tests (Room test, Ojai test)
- The vocabulary Margot uses / doesn't use
- The banned words list
- Always does / never does
- One closing filter question

### Why it worked

The original doc tried to be inspiring. The compressed doc is operational. **An agency builds the first. A cult brand builds the second.** The inspiring version lives in the archive for people who want depth. The operational version gets used on Tuesday at 3pm when someone has to write a subject line.

### Before/after opening

**Before (157 words):**
> This document is not a brand personality guide. It's deeper than that. This is the complete profile of Margot Cole — the woman who is Small Plates. She is not a customer persona. She is not a target demographic. She is not the bride, the MOH, or the mom. She is the brand. When the bride discovers Small Plates, she sees Margot and thinks: this brand gets me. When the MOH discovers Small Plates, she sees Margot and thinks: I want to be the person who gives this. When the mom discovers Small Plates, she sees Margot and thinks: this is exactly what my daughter would love — and honestly, so do I. One person. Every audience finds herself in her.

**After (38 words):**
> *The filter. Not a persona. Not a customer. She is the brand's taste — the person every line of copy, every image, every decision has to pass through.*
>
> *When in doubt, ask: "Would Margot love it, ignore it, or find it cringe?"*

Same content. One-fourth the words. The reader knows immediately what Margot is for, not just what she is.

## Example 2 — The Brand Idea doc

**Original doc:** ~2,800 words. Four explanations of the same idea.

**Compressed doc:** ~400 words. One explanation, four articulations.

**Reduction:** 86%.

### What was cut

- Four separate explanations of "why presence, not memory" → reduced to one three-line table
- "Five Criteria" rationalizing why the idea is good → removed (internal validation, not tool)
- "Stress Test" section → removed (academic demonstration)
- Decision Framework tables (~15 rows of trivial Q&A) → removed (answers deducible from the idea itself)
- Language Guide (extensive word lists) → migrated to voice-anatomy
- ASCII diagram → removed (redundant with the table above it)
- Summary section → removed (a 400-word doc doesn't need a summary)

### What was kept

- The idea in one line
- The single north-star question
- The three-line Memory vs Presence table
- The four articulations (strategic / shorthand / poetic / functional)
- The Organizer Layer
- Connection to other docs

### Why it worked

The original doc was defending the idea. The compressed doc *applies* the idea. Once a team believes in a strategic concept, the team doesn't need to re-justify it every time someone opens the doc — they need to know how to use it today.

### Before/after of a typical section

**Before (128 words):**
> Why "Presence" and Not "Memory"
>
> Presence is more than memory. Memory is past-tense. Presence is now.
>
> Small Plates doesn't help you remember your wedding. It helps you continue it. The people who were at your table are still at your table. That's the difference.
>
> The longer version is the center for one reason: it names the wound directly. "Vivid Presence" is a label. Useful internally as shorthand, but it requires explanation. You can't hand it to someone on the team and expect them to know what to do with it without context. "The people who matter to you shouldn't disappear" doesn't need explanation. It's the thing itself. Everyone who hears it knows exactly what it means — because they've felt it. That's the standard a Brand Idea should meet.

**After (42 words):**
> ## PRESENCE, NOT MEMORY
>
> The entire category confuses these. We don't.
>
> | | Tense | What it says |
> |---|---|---|
> | Memory | Past | "They were there" |
> | Keepsake | Past | "This reminds me of them" |
> | **Presence** | **Present** | **"They are here"** |

Same insight. One-third the words. The table does the teaching that three paragraphs used to do.

## Example 3 — The Foundation doc

**Original state:** Scattered across 10 separate docs (personality, POV, manifesto, enemy, positioning, tagline, tone, visual, messaging, idea).

**Compressed state:** One foundation.md, ~400 words, covering belief + enemy + audience + promise + always/never + filter.

**Reduction:** ~95% of the original total word count across all brand docs.

### The core insight of this compression

**Brand docs mostly repeat each other.** A "manifesto" doc often says the same thing as a "POV" doc with different vocabulary. A "personality" doc overlaps with "tone of voice" by 70%. The reader reads all of them and gets confused because the brand is saying five versions of similar things in slightly different words.

The compression method: **identify the true atomic components** (belief, enemy, audience, promise, filter, rules), put each in exactly one doc, and archive everything else. Ten docs become three: foundation.md, brand-idea.md, margot.md.

## Pattern recognition — what to always cut

Across every compression, these categories of content should be cut by default:

1. **Meta-commentary about the doc itself.** *"This document is not just a guide, it's…"* Cut. The reader will assess what it is.

2. **Rationalization for strategic choices the reader already accepts.** If the founder approved the belief, stop selling the belief inside the brand docs. Just state it.

3. **Multiple versions of the same point with different emphasis.** Pick the strongest version. Cut the others.

4. **Trivial Q&A tables.** "Should the book be designed to last? Yes." If the answer is deducible from the core principle, don't spell it out. The team will get it.

5. **Historical discussion (v1 vs v2, old positioning vs new).** Put this in a footer or archive. It's not operational.

6. **Five Criteria / Stress Tests / Framework Validations.** These are pitch-deck content, not operational-doc content.

7. **ASCII diagrams or flowcharts that repeat a table or paragraph.** Redundant visuals are still redundancy.

8. **Summaries at the end of short docs.** A 400-word doc doesn't summarize itself. A 4,000-word doc shouldn't exist in the first place.

---

## Pattern recognition — what to always keep

1. **The core belief in one sentence.** Always. This is the spine.
2. **A single north-star question the reader can apply.** Preferably under 10 words.
3. **Before/after polarity (what we are NOT, vs what we are).** Usually one table, 3-5 rows.
4. **Operational rules (always / never).** Not philosophies. Rules.
5. **One test the reader can run.** "Would Margot say this?" "Does this keep people from disappearing?"
6. **Examples, if they clarify the rule.** Always concrete. Never generic.

## The compression workflow (replicate this)

When compressing any brand doc, run this sequence:

1. **Read the whole doc once, no edits.** Note where it says the same thing twice.
2. **Identify the atomic components.** What is the one thing this doc is actually about?
3. **Extract the operational content.** What produces decisions?
4. **Kill justification.** Anything that argues *why* the reader should believe the thesis → archive.
5. **Kill redundancy.** Anything said twice → keep the strongest version.
6. **Kill the last sentence of every section.** First draft always has explanation hiding there.
7. **Rewrite openings to be blunt, not literary.** *"This doc is not just a guide…"* → *"This governs everything."*
8. **Archive the original.** Never delete. The deep version has value for someone who wants to reconstruct the reasoning later.
9. **Footer the compressed version with a version note** (*v1.4 · April 2026 · changes from v1.3: …*). This preserves history without bloating the doc.

---

*These examples are real. The math (89%, 86%, 95%) is real. The method works because brand docs start verbose by default — agencies are paid by the deliverable, not the decision, so more words is more value. Cult brands operate in the opposite direction: fewer words, sharper decisions, more ship-worthy outputs.*
