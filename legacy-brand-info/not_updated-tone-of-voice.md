# How We Sound

---

## The Voice

Small Plates sounds like the friend who always knows — and says it before you ask.

Knows the restaurant you should try.
Knows the gift that will actually land.
Knows when something is wrong with your plan, and tells you.

She doesn't soften it.
She doesn't wait for the right moment.
She just says it — because she respects you enough to be honest.

Not loud. Not harsh.
Just certain. And a little ahead of everyone else.

The kind of voice that makes you lean in,
not because she's whispering,
but because what she's saying is true
and nobody else was going to say it.

---

## The Formula

This is the rule that governs everything:

> **Cool on the outside. Emotional on the inside.**

Not the reverse. Never the reverse.

The surface is confident, stylish, effortless.
The core is deeply human, genuinely caring, emotionally real.

Think of it like the book itself:
- **The cover:** Minimal. Designed. Something you want on display.
- **The pages:** Handwritten notes. Family recipes. Names that make you cry.

The voice works the same way.

We don't lead with emotion. We earn it.
We don't beg for attention. We deserve it.
We don't explain why this matters. We show it.

---

## The Five Principles

Every piece of Small Plates communication should feel like these five things — in different proportions depending on context and audience.

---

### 1. Warm, With Edge

We care. Genuinely.
But we're not soft about it.

Warmth doesn't mean gentle.
It means present. Real. Human.

We're the friend who shows up with food when you're sad —
and tells you when you're being ridiculous *before* you make the mistake, not after.

Warmth doesn't protect you from the truth.
It's what makes the truth land without damage.

**Warm with edge** = Love that doesn't coddle.

We sound like:
- Someone who actually knows you
- A friend, not a brand
- Someone who names the thing nobody's naming
- Honest, even when it's easier not to be
- A friend who says "honestly? skip it" — and you know they're right

We don't sound like:
- A greeting card
- Customer service
- Someone trying too hard to be liked
- Someone who softens until the point disappears

---

### 2. Direct, Not Blunt

We say what we mean.
First sentence. No warmup.

But direct doesn't mean harsh.
It means we respect your time.
We get to the point because the point is worth getting to.

**Direct, not blunt** = Clarity with care.

We sound like:
- Someone confident in what they're saying
- Efficient without being cold
- Clear, even about emotional things

We don't sound like:
- A press release
- A legal disclaimer
- Someone who uses ten words when three would do

---

### 3. Effortless, Not Lazy

We make it look easy.
That's the skill.

The writing flows. The rhythm feels natural.
But underneath, every word is chosen.

**Effortless, not lazy** = The craft is invisible.

We sound like:
- Someone who's naturally good with words
- Cool without trying
- Smart without showing off

We don't sound like:
- Overly casual or sloppy
- Trying to be "relatable"
- Like we didn't care enough to edit

---

### 4. Moving, Not Pushing

We create momentum.
We make people want to act.

But we never pressure. Never guilt. Never create fake urgency.

The energy comes from excitement, not anxiety.

**Moving, not pushing** = Forward motion without force.

We sound like:
- Someone with a good idea who wants to share it
- A brand that makes you want to move
- Energizing without being exhausting

We don't sound like:
- A countdown timer
- A sales pitch
- Someone who needs you to buy this right now

---

### 5. Say the Quiet Part

The wedding industry is afraid of the truth.
We're not.

Not to provoke. Not to stand out.
But because we have enough confidence to be honest
when everyone else is being careful.

Most gifts end up in a drawer — and everyone knows it.
Most wedding keepsakes get one look and disappear — and everyone knows it.
A cookbook sounds like something your aunt would give you — and that's the exact thing we have to earn our way out of.

We say these things.
Not to be contrarian. Not to make anyone uncomfortable.
But because naming the real thing is the only way to offer something real instead.

**Say the quiet part** = Name what everyone's thinking and nobody's saying.

We sound like:
- A brand that doesn't need to protect the industry it's in
- Someone who finally says what's in the room
- Honest without being harsh. Direct without being rude.
- The person who says "most wedding gifts end up in a drawer" — and then shows you why this one won't

We don't sound like:
- A brand that plays it safe to avoid offending anyone
- Provocative for the sake of attention
- Someone who softens the truth until it disappears
- Edgy without substance

## Tone by Audience

The voice stays the same.
The tone adjusts based on who we're talking to and what they need to hear.

---

### The Bride — As Gift Receiver

**Who she is:**
- 27-34, has seen every wedding on Instagram
- Exhausted by generic, terrified of cringe
- Wants her wedding to feel like *her*

**What she fears:**
Something feeling basic, predictable, or cheesy.

**What she needs to hear:**
"This is different. This is cool. This belongs in your kitchen, not a closet."

**How we sound to her:**
Confident. Stylish. Zero drama. Like recommending a restaurant we know she'll love.

**Example — Instagram ad:**
> Your wedding registry is full of things you'll return.
>
> This isn't one of them.
>
> It's a book made by the people who showed up.
> And it lives in your kitchen.
> Where you'll actually see it.

**Who she is:**
- Same woman, different mode
- She wants to give her guests something meaningful to do
- She wants her wedding to feel participatory, not transactional

Guests feeling like spectators. The wedding being "just another wedding."

### The Organizer (MOH / Bridesmaid / Sister / Best Friend)

**Who she is:**
- The one actually buying and managing this
- Already carrying 14 things for this wedding
- Wants to be the hero without becoming the martyr
- Needs this to be handled, not just "easy"

**Her three fears:**
- **Ineptitude:** "What if this falls apart and it's my fault?"
- **Subjugation:** "What if this becomes another thing where I do all the work?"
- **Depletion:** "What if I don't have the bandwidth for one more task?"

**What she fears is NOT:**
"Will this work?" — she's smarter than that.
Her real fear: "Will this become MY problem?"

**What she needs to hear:**
"You're managing 14 things for this wedding. This isn't one of them. You set it up. We handle the rest. Check the dashboard when you want — but you don't have to."

**How we sound to her:**
COO to COO. Operational respect. Not "you've got this" — more like "it's handled."

**Example — Landing page for organizers:**
> You're managing enough for this wedding.
> This isn't another thing to manage.
>
> Here's what you do:
>
> 1. Set up the book (5 minutes).
> 2. Invite the guests (we give you the link).
> 3. That's it. We send reminders. We design it. We print it.
>
> You check the dashboard when you want.
> You don't chase anyone.
> Bride cries. You're the hero.
>
> [Start the book →]

**Example — Dashboard notification:**
> 28 of 42 recipes in. 3 reminders sent.
> Everything's on track. Nothing for you to do.

## The Energy Scale

Different moments need different energy. Here's the range:

| Context | Energy Level | Feels Like |
|---------|--------------|------------|
| Guest reminders | High | "Come on, let's go, you've got this" |
| Organizer dashboard | Medium | "Status report from a trusted partner" |
| Homepage | Medium-High | "This is good, let me show you" |
| How it works | Medium | "Here's the deal, super simple" |
| Bride-as-creator flow | Medium-High | "You're building something amazing" |
| Emotional sections | Medium-Low | "This is why it matters" |
| Packaging | Low | "Let it land" |
| Post-delivery | Low-Medium | "You did it. Enjoy this." |

The key: we can go low-energy, but we should never go passive. Even quiet moments have intention.

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do

- **Start strong.** First sentence earns the second.
- **Name the uncomfortable thing.** The truth about wedding gifts, about keepsakes, about what people actually use — say it before they think it. That's what builds trust.
- **End with momentum.** Last line should make them want to act or feel something.
- **Use "you."** We're talking to someone, not at them.
- **Be specific.** "Your mom's pasta" > "a family recipe."
- **Make it easy.** Reduce friction in language. "5 minutes. Done."
- **Sound certain.** "This is good" > "We think you'll like this."
- **Mix short and long.** Rhythm matters.
- **Read it out loud.** If it sounds like a brand, rewrite it.
- **Speak to the organizer as an equal.** She's not a customer to reassure. She's a peer to respect.

### Don't

- **Don't hedge.** No "maybe," "perhaps," "we think."
- **Don't over-explain.** Trust the reader.
- **Don't use exclamation points.** Almost never. Confidence doesn't shout.
- **Don't start with "We're excited..."** We're not a press release.
- **Don't be passive.** Even emotional moments have forward motion.
- **Don't guilt or pressure.** Energy yes, anxiety no.
- **Don't use emojis.** Exception: Stories, sparingly.
- **Don't sacrifice clarity for style.** If it's cool but confusing, rewrite.
- **Don't soften until there's nothing left.** Edge is not cruelty. It's honesty.
- **Don't reassure when you should respect.** The organizer doesn't need "don't worry." She needs "it's handled."

## The Gut Check

Before publishing anything, ask two questions:

**Question 1:**
> "Would our 28-year-old New York woman, champagne in hand on a rooftop, say this?"

If it sounds like something she'd roll her eyes at — rewrite.
If it sounds like something she'd say with a knowing smile — ship it.
If it sounds like something she'd say that makes everyone at the table go quiet for a second — even better.

**Question 2:**
> "Does this make someone want to do something?"

Not pressure. Not guilt. Want.

If the answer is no, add momentum.
If the answer is yes, you're good.
