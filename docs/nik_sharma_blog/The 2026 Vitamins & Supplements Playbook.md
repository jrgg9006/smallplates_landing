Why the supplement category looks different in 2026
Two things happened between 2022 and now.

 

Privacy-first attribution killed easy Meta ROAS. iOS 14.5 and cookie deprecation pushed CAC up and made attribution qualitative. Winners moved budget into brand, education, and retention. Losers kept pushing performance into a broken pipe and went under.

 

Then GLP-1 reshaped every adjacent category. Weight management became "GLP-1 companion support." Mood became "Ozempic personality" whitespace that nobody has claimed yet. Cortisol, metabolic, appetite, adaptogens... all of it got re-contextualized through the lens of "I'm on a shot and I need help with the rest."

 

There's also a quieter shift underneath all of it. The brands that win today are back to thinking in LTV, not just CAC. For a few years, post-pandemic, the whole industry got drunk on top-line growth and creative volume tests. One UGC video, ten variations, optimize the hook, scale CAC down. That worked when meta was cheap. It doesn't anymore. The brands actually compounding right now are the ones who quietly went back to the old LTV math while everyone else was chasing the next ad format.

 

The brands winning didn't invent a better multivitamin. They collapsed the whole stack, ad to education to LP to sub to retention, into one coherent machine. Most brands in the category are still running five disconnected tools and calling it marketing.

 

Here's the playbook.

1. Ad-to-LP congruency (the ALO framework)
The single biggest unforced error in this category is running a killer ad and sending the click to a homepage that doesn't match. I see it constantly.

 

The framework I use to think about the modern paid funnel is ALO. Ad, Lander, Offer. Audience used to be the fourth letter (the old ACE / TRACE models). With Andromeda, Meta is doing so much of the targeting work that audience is basically a downstream output of the creative now... if your ad clearly signals who it's for, Meta will find them. So your job is the other three. Ad, Lander, Offer. All three congruent. All three locked.

 

The rule is simple. The ad hook becomes the LP headline. Literally the same words. The ad hero image becomes the LP hero image. Literally the same frame. If you're running 5 ad angles, you build 5 landing pages. Not 1 LP and 5 ads.

 

Andromeda actually rewards this even harder than the old system did. Meta now wants to see 25 completely different ads, not one ad with 25 variations. An ad for fiber. An ad for menopause. An ad for sleep. An ad for gym performance. Each one matched to its own page that continues the same promise.

 

Look at AG1. The Meta ad has Hugh Jackman. The LP has Hugh Jackman. Same frame. Same glass. Same pour. Marketing Examples called it "idiot-proof." That's the compliment. It's so obviously aligned the customer never has to translate. The AG1 landing page is converting at 9.5%. That's not a typo. Nine and a half percent on cold paid traffic, because the click-to-LP experience never makes the buyer translate between two different promises.

 

Look at Gruns. Ad hook: "less expensive than AG1 and much easier to take." LP hero headline: "less expensive than AG1 and much easier to take." Same pinned testimonial above the fold on both. 85,000+ reviews doing social math in the corner. That's why their conversion is what it is.

 

Now look at IM8. $108M on Shopify. Beckham on every ad. And the ads dump to a homepage. No Beckham LP. No energy LP. No cognition LP. No longevity LP. Hayes Media flagged this as the single biggest unforced error in the category, and I agree. They're leaving 15 to 30% conversion lift on the table every day they run it.

 

The 10 funnel patterns that are working right now:

Quiz-gated intent funnels (Hers, Ro, Hims, peptide brands). The quiz also doubles as a Meta tracking workaround for health categories... certain answers become positive intent signals you pass back.
Founder/origin UGC (Ritual, IM8, Gruns).
Doctor or scientist endorsement front-to-back (Momentous with Huberman, David Protein, Bonafide with Dr. Alyssa).
Competitor comparison ads / comparacles (Gruns vs AG1, David vs Barebells, Huel vs MyProtein). MSA's ad library is full of these for a reason.
Problem, agitation, solution UGC (Love Wellness, Lemme). Hand-out before pitch.
Ingredient-led mechanism visual (Thorne, LMNT, Armra, Array).
Advertorial-native (Caraway built the formula, every supplement and telemedicine brand now runs it). 15 to 40% of ad spend in the category.
Stat-led editorial (Seed, Thorne, Legion). Real clinical data carrying the lead.
Creator video with promo code (lower-funnel retargeting psychology... "everyone is talking about this brand").
Memes. Smaller share, but they ship.
Whatever pattern you pick, the rule doesn't change: the ad has to match the lander has to match the offer. Generative AI is the reason this is finally affordable. With Claude Code, Codex, and Manus, building 10 matched landing pages is a one-day job, not a six-week sprint. There is no excuse to run a fiber ad into a homepage anymore.


Your move: Pull up your top 5 ad creatives. Click each one. If the LP you land on doesn't repeat the ad hook word-for-word in the H1, fix that before you spend another dollar.

2. Ailment-specific landing pages (one page per problem)
Related but different. The brands winning 2026 build a dedicated landing page for every concern they serve. Hero is the problem, not the product. Testimonials filtered to that concern. Creative matched to that LP.

 

Love Wellness runs the purest version of this. Their SKU names ARE the ailment. Bye Bye Bloat. The Killer. Good Girl Probiotics. Sparkle Fiber. Zero cognitive load. You see the ad about bloating, you click, you land on the Bye Bye Bloat page, you buy Bye Bye Bloat. The whole funnel collapsed into one word.

 

Gruns built a "GLP-1 user greens" variant LP. Narrow audience, narrow message, narrow LP. It prints.

 

Perelel routes every single ad into a 60-second quiz that sorts you into trimester-specific PDPs. 1st trimester, 2nd, 3rd, postnatal. Four different LPs. Four different creatives. Perfect congruency by architecture.

 

The quiz-as-front-door isn't a vibe call, it's a structural one. A multi-SKU brand sending paid traffic to a catalog page is asking the customer to do the sorting. A quiz does the sorting for them, captures intent on the way through, and lands them on a PDP that already matches their answer. It also doubles as a tracking workaround in regulated categories... certain quiz answers can flow back to Meta as positive intent signals when your pixel can't do the heavy lifting. Worth noting: Octane (the dominant quiz tool) just got acquired by Meta, which means quiz funnels are about to get more Meta-native, not less. Build now.

 

Bonafide owns perimenopause and built individual symptom LPs for hot flashes, sleep, mood, vaginal dryness. Each one stands alone. Each one converts its own traffic.


Your move: Pick one concern you can credibly own. Write the LP for it this week. Run 3 ad variants against it next week. That's a full campaign.

3. The PDP is a classroom, not a checkout
This is the section I'd put on a billboard if I could.

 

Every winning VMS brand in 2026 treats the product page as an education asset, not a transaction screen. The brands losing on LTV are the ones rushing customers through with timers, scarcity overlays, and shoved bundles. They spend the next 90 days fighting churn because they sold a product nobody understood.

 

Educate. Don't trick. The brands focused on education win on LTV. The brands focused on velocity to checkout win one purchase, then burn out.

 

The image carousel as a mini landing page.

 

Every brand I audited had at least 5 images. The best run 8 to 10. Heat-map data says most users get through 5 or 6, but if they're invested, they keep scrolling. Treat the carousel like a landing page in 10 frames:

Hero product shot with text overlay and icons
Lifestyle shot... celebrity, founder, or UGC creator using the product
Ingredient or benefit infographic (mini-listicle of the 8 ingredients or 9 benefits)
How-it-works visual: scoop, shake, drink. With timeline... "by week 2, you should feel X. By day 30, you should feel Y."
Credentialing card. Doctor quote, Mayo Clinic logo, NSF badge, clinical trial result. Third-party only, not your own brand claims.
Welcome kit flat lay (if you have one)
Competitor comparison chart
Founder photo with the why-I-started-this quote
Okendo review collage on top of lifestyle photography
UGC video or another infographic
This is the Jolie / NeuroGum / MediCube TikTok Shop playbook applied to your DTC PDP. Most people don't scroll deep on product pages. So you put the landing page in the carousel.

 

Above the fold, you must have:

Star rating and review count next to the product title (you would not believe how many brands skip this)
Supplement / nutritional panel with dosages
Where possible, dosage callouts that match clinical trials. "200mg magnesium glycinate (clinical dosage for sleep onset)." That parenthetical does more conversion lift than any badge.
Expert endorsement above the fold
Buy box defaulted to subscribe, with a clear delta vs. one-time
Subscription price framed per day. "Less than $2/day" reads better than "$55/month." This is supplement girl-math and it works.
Strikethrough one-time vs subscribe pricing
Trust bar below the buy box: third-party tested, money-back guarantee, no artificial colors, whatever applies
Quantity or variant selector (skip if you sell starter packs)
Risk reversal line. "30-day money-back guarantee." Magic Spoon's "happiness guarantee" is the gold version.
Below the fold, you need 10 modules:

Who is this for. "If you're a woman in her 30s dealing with afternoon brain fog, this is for you." Spell it out like you're explaining to a 10-year-old.
The problem. One sentence. "77% of Americans take a supplement, but 90% remain deficient."
How the product solves it. Plain English. No jargon.
Ingredient breakdown with dosage and citation.
Clinical trial module. Yours if you have it, ingredient-level if you don't.
Comparison chart (you vs the obvious competitor).
Timeline to results. This is the module that prevents the most cancellations. "Week 1: nothing. Week 2: first signs. Week 6: full effect." Customers cancel because the product didn't match the expectation. Set the expectation, save the customer.
Social proof layer. Reviews, UGC video.
FAQs with real objections. Not your stock FAQ. The actual questions your CX team is answering on Gorgias.
Final CTA with guarantee.
Mobile-first or you've already lost. 70 to 85% of your traffic is mobile. If you designed for desktop and crammed it down to mobile, the customer can feel it. Horizontal swipe with dot indicators. Sticky add-to-cart. Carts that upsell. UGC videos that play in-page, not in the iOS native player. Real alt text. Compressed file sizes. It's 2026. Mobile-first stopped being a recommendation in 2017.


Your move: Open your top-selling PDP on your phone right now. Count the carousel images. Count the modules below the fold. If you don't have all 10 modules and at least 8 carousel slots, you have a weekend project. This single fix usually moves PDP CR by 20 to 40%.

4. 90-day paid-upfront subscriptions (the payback accelerator)
This is the offer-architecture move that is quietly reshaping what brands can afford to pay for acquisition.

 

The math, quickly. A $79/month sub at 25% gross margin is $19.75 of gross profit in month one. At a $150 CAC (Love Wellness's historical number), that's an 8-month payback. Brutal on cash flow. Basically forces you to choose between growth and profitability.

 

Now take the same brand at $237 prepaid for a 90-day sub. Same margin, you just collected $59.25 of gross profit on day one. Payback drops from 8 months to 2.5. That means you can outspend a monthly-only competitor by 2 to 3x on paid acquisition. Same product. Same customer. Different cash mechanics.

 

The fulfillment side of this is underrated too. One quarterly shipment costs you one box of shipping and one round of credit card processing fees. Three monthly shipments costs you three. Triple-tax on payment processing, triple-tax on logistics. The 90-day sub is a margin gift before the customer ever feels the discount.

 

IM8 doubled their AOV from $110 to $220 the day they pushed quarterly at 40% off as the default. Mud\Wtr sells a $360 annual plan as its own SKU. "$1 per serving." 3 months free framing. Seed runs 10 to 15% additional off for their 3-month and 6-month Sustainable Refill Program. Needed runs "Save $100 on your first 3 months."

 

The structure I recommend across every consumable brand I work with:

One-time: full price, product only
30-day sub: 15% off, product only. This tier exists as the visible anchor.
90-day sub: 30% off + free shipping + a PDF gift (protocol guide, recipe book, workout plan). Zero COGS on the PDF, high perceived value. This is the default selection on the PDP.
Annual: 40% off + VIP priority CX. Unlock this after you have 5,000+ reviews. Not before.
Default on the PDP is subscribe. One-time is a secondary toggle. The 30-day exists to make the 90-day look like the obvious deal. Classic decoy. It works every time.

 

One more thing. Nik's rule on trial packs... the SheMD data showed an $8 trial pack converts 95 to 98% of buyers to subscription. Your 10% off does not. People don't want a discount on a commitment. They want a low-risk way to start.


Your move: Add a 90-day SKU to your PDP this week. Price it at 25 to 30% off monthly. Stack a PDF gift. Default-select it. See what happens to AOV in the next 30 days.

5. The welcome kit / first-order offer
40 to 50% of your funnel lives here. The offer is the moment the buyer decides whether they made a good decision or a regrettable one. The brands winning 2026 treat the first-order experience as theatre.

 

What I see working:

Free Tumblr or shaker bottle (gives the customer a daily ritual object with your logo on it, free brand impressions every morning)
Travel tin for capsules (turns the product into something they pack)
Recipe guide or protocol PDF (zero COGS, high perceived value, doubles as education)
Three-month subscription to Calm, Othership, or a similar app (cross-category status play, particularly strong for sleep/cortisol brands)
Sweepstakes entry (every subscriber entered to win a car, a watch, a vacation)
Free product addition (extra sample, second SKU, travel size)
The combination that consistently outperforms is product + 2 small gifts + meaningful discount, not one big discount. Discounts read as "this is what the product is worth." Gifts read as "they want me to win." Different psychology. Same dollars.

 

The welcome kit is also where you set the second purchase up. The PDF is education. The Tumblr is identity. The protocol is reason-to-stay. Don't waste the box.


Your move: Audit your current first-order experience. If a customer's box arrives with the product and a generic thank-you card, you have nothing memorable. Add one zero-COGS gift (PDF) and one branded ritual object (Tumblr, tin, sticker pack) and watch your second-order rate move.

6. Post-purchase education (10 touchpoints, not 3)
The brands who are running post-purchase like it's still 2019 are losing 30 to 40% of possible LTV. Most brands send 3 emails after a purchase. Order confirmation. Shipped. Delivered. Then silence.

 

The winners send 10.

 

The Feastables + Mud\Wtr playbook looks like this:

Day 0: Order confirmation, in brand voice, not stock Shopify
Day 3: Shipping notification written like a person
Day 4: Content drop before delivery (Mud\Wtr sends a full docu-film called "Change Your Mind" right here)
Day 5: Out for delivery + delivered
Day 6 to 10: How-to-use content
Day 14: First expectation-setter... "you should notice X by now"
Day 30: Second expectation-setter... "week 4 check-in"
Day 66: Habit-formation email
Day 90: Review request + sub conversion for one-time buyers
That Day 66 email is Seed's signature and it's the category's secret weapon. The copy references the actual neuroscience... 66 days is the average for habit formation in adults. Seed tells the customer "your brain just locked this in, you made it." The email reinforces the ritual at the exact neurological moment it becomes automatic. Nobody else in the category runs a behavior-triggered cadence timed to neuroscience.

 

Why this matters. Customers don't churn because the product stopped working. They churn because reality didn't match their expectation. They saw the ad, imagined the outcome, bought the product, didn't feel the miracle in 48 hours, canceled.

 

The fix is to tell them the truth before they buy AND to keep telling them the truth after. "You'll notice the first change in week two. Most customers feel the full effect around week six." That sentence on the PDP and again in the Day 14 email saves a cancel-flow conversation in month three.

 

Your move: Audit your current post-purchase sequence. Count the emails. If it's under 7, you have work to do. Start with adding a Day 14 expectation-setter and a Day 66 habit-formation email. Two emails. Will measurably move your churn number.

7. Cancel-save ladders (skip before discount)
Here's an uncomfortable truth. Most brands I audit have no cancel-save flow at all. Customer clicks cancel, customer is gone. One click. No friction. No save. And then the founder wonders why retention looks the way it does.

 

Then there's the other extreme... brands that make canceling so hard it's borderline illegal. Don't do that either. The FTC is finally catching up, and even where the law isn't biting yet, the chargebacks and the reviews will. The point of a save flow is not to trap people. It's to give them options they didn't know they had.

 

The winning ladder is 5 steps and the discount is LAST, not first:

Skip this shipment. Primary CTA. Bigger button. "Not quite ready? Skip one shipment." Skip saves at 40%+ vs cancel.
Change frequency. "Getting too much? Switch to every 60 days." Higher save rate than any discount.
Swap SKU. "Try the smaller pack." Or "Move to the Longevity stack." Conversion happens here often.
Pause. "Pause for 30 days, nothing happens until you're ready."
Discount save. Last resort. 20 to 25% off next order. Use this only when the first four fail.
The gold standard in the category is Seed. Their cancel flow includes a structured exit survey that captures feedback BEFORE letting the customer go. The survey isn't just a save mechanic. It's a product R&D loop. Seed used exit data ("I have too much product left") to redesign their entire Sustainable Refill Program. The cancel flow literally became an R&D pipeline.

 

Borrow from outside the category too. Wall Street Journal's cancel flow offers you a full month for $1 to stay. They know that getting the customer to agree to one more billable month, even at $1, has a high probability of turning into three more months at full price. SaaS and newspapers have been running this play for two decades. Supplements is finally catching up.

 

The other pattern worth borrowing is a real post-cancel recovery series. Most brands send one "sorry to see you go" email and stop. The brands compounding LTV run a multi-week sequence after cancellation that's written like a relationship, not a coupon. The frame is identity, not price. Discount is there if the customer wants it, but the copy is pulling them back on who they are when they take the product, not on $5 off.

 

Hiya's version is even more mechanical. The kid is attached to the refillable bottle with the stickers on it. Every month a new sticker pack arrives in the box. Canceling the subscription means disappointing the kid. That's not marketing. That's product design as retention.


Your move: If your cancel flow is one click and a confirmation page, you're leaving real money on the table. Build the 5-step ladder this week. Skip before discount. Add an exit survey and read the responses.

8. Named MD panels over celebrity cameos
In 2022, the play was get a celebrity on the can. Hugh Jackman for AG1. Beckham for IM8. Serena for Ritual. Kourtney for Lemme. That worked when the celebrity had a real product story.

 

In 2026, the play is get a scientific advisory board, an MD panel, or a single named practitioner that's deeply integrated into the product. Named. Credentialed. Photographed. Institutional.

 

Momentous has Huberman. Not as a paid endorser. As a protocol partner. He publishes actual protocols that call for their specific SKUs at specific doses. David Protein has him as a founding advisor and leads with it in every ad. That's the category move.

 

IM8 built a scientific advisory board with Mayo Clinic, NASA, and Cedars-Sinai names attached. That is trust AG1 cannot replicate with Hugh Jackman alone. Hugh is a movie star. Mayo is Mayo.

 

Hiya runs Dr. Joel Warsh. Bonafide runs Dr. Alyssa Dweck. Needed runs Dr. Pero. Perelel shows a full panel of OBGYNs with photos and institutions. GoodOnya layers in Front Row MD's "Clinician's Choice" badge with 40+ doctors behind it. Venus Williams' protein brand went a step further and embedded a Front Row clinician AI chat right on the PDP... real-time questions about ingredients, drug interactions, dosages, all backed by clinician data. That's where the category is going.

 

The reason this works... health-savvy consumers trust doctors more than movie stars. And MD panels are a fraction of the cost of a celebrity deal.

 

The contrast worth studying is Naomi Watts with Stripes Beauty. On paper it looks like another celebrity deal. The reason it actually converts is Naomi Watts has menopause. She talks about it publicly. She uses the product. The story is real because the person is real to the category. That's the bar.

 

The rule. Celebrity drives traffic. Authentic story drives conversion. If your celebrity doesn't have a genuine product story, you're buying awareness, not sales. Build the MD panel instead.


Your move: If you can afford one celebrity deal, stop. Take the same budget and hire 4 to 6 doctors with photos, credentials, and institutional affiliations. Put them above the fold. Watch your trust signal and your conversion rate both go up.

9. Content factory at volume (500 pieces, not one $10K shoot)
The old UGC model was you pay an agency $10K and they give you 80 videos. Polish, lighting, direction, studio. That model is dead.

 

The new model is Comfrt. They run a creator network in the hundreds of thousands and ship hundreds of pieces of content a day. Not a week. A day. The math isn't close. 80 polished videos vs 500 raw videos... the raw volume wins every single time because it hits the algorithm, variety beats polish, and you can test 50 hooks before a polished brand would ship one. The mechanic underneath it is what matters: ship at volume organically, watch the data, identify the videos that perform above your baseline, then put paid spend behind only those. The creators do the testing for you. You just amplify the winners.

 

David Protein leans into the Huberman integration across hundreds of organic creator clips where the creator is referencing a specific episode. LMNT effectively bought podcast monopoly over the paleo/keto/endurance category. If you're running on a treadmill in 2026 and you listen to a performance podcast, you heard an LMNT ad. And every host has a personalized code. LMNT/rogan. LMNT/huberman. LMNT/attia. Different URL, different promo code, different attribution, same product.

 

The feedback loop is what makes the content factory work. You're not just making more ads. You're collecting more data. Every piece of content gets tagged by hook, angle, ailment, format, performance. The ones that win get scaled. The ones that lose get retired. And the best-performing ones get remade 10 different ways.

 

Comfrt also does something smart. They run Meta ads specifically to recruit new affiliates (30% off signup, 3,000 to 4,000 new affiliates per day). The acquisition isn't end-customer. The acquisition is more creators. More creators = more content = more attribution-traceable sales.

 

The rule. You need a creator network before you scale paid. Not after. Not simultaneously. Before. Because the content factory is the acquisition engine, and paid is just the amplifier.


Your move: Count how many pieces of new creator content your brand shipped last month. If it's under 30, you don't have a content factory, you have a bottleneck. Start by recruiting 20 micro-creators with no upfront retainer, ship 60 videos next month, watch the data, and only put paid behind the ones that beat your organic baseline. Then 100. Then 500.
