# The Foundation

## The Brand Idea

**Vivid Presence**

The people who love you stay in your life — not as memories, but as presence.

Every color, every typeface, every photograph, every layout decision answers one question:

> **"Does this make the presence more vivid?"**

If the answer is yes, proceed.
If the answer is no, reconsider.

---

## The Key Insight

**The book is the vehicle. Presence is the product.**

Every image says: these people are in your kitchen, even when they can't be.

Not warm and aspirational. **Real and unexpected.**

---

## The Formula

> **Cool on the outside. Emotional on the inside.**

The surface is confident, stylish, effortless. The core is deeply human, genuinely caring, emotionally real.

We don't lead with emotion. We earn it. We don't beg for attention. We deserve it. We don't explain why this matters. We show it.

## The Generation We're Designing For

*This section replaces the previous "Woman We're Designing For." The brand has sharpened its focus.*

### The Shift

The millennial wedding was a production. Pinterest boards. 200 guests. The perfect day.

Gen Z's wedding is a gathering. Forty people who actually matter. Backyard, barn, rooftop. The day is real, not performed.

**Small Plates was built for the millennial wedding but belongs to the Gen Z one.**

The product is more aligned with Gen Z values than any wedding brand that's actually targeting them. Our job is to close that gap in how we show up — visually, tonally, and culturally.

### The Bride (Gen Z)

She's planned less of her wedding on Pinterest and more of it on TikTok. She cares deeply about who's in the room. She finds most wedding industry marketing slightly embarrassing — too much, too soft, too performed. She wants her wedding to feel like her, not like *a wedding*.

**What she needs from us:** To feel seen without being pandered to. To find a product that has taste, without a brand that lectures her about it.

> *Design test: Would she show this to her friends without explanation? If she has to say "it's actually really cool" — the design failed.*

### The MOH (The Real Activator)

She is the most important person in Small Plates' universe. Not because she buys the product — because she *makes it happen*. She's the one who coordinates, reminds, follows up, and delivers. Without her, there is no book.

She needs to feel two things simultaneously:
1. **This is cool enough to represent me.**
2. **This is easy enough to actually pull off.**

If Small Plates feels like something her mom would organize, she won't organize it. She has to feel like *she discovered this* — that she's the person in the group who finds the things nobody else finds.

**What she needs from us:** To feel like a protagonist, not an intermediary. The product should feel like an extension of her taste, not a service she's completing for someone else.

> *Design test: Would a 26-year-old who found us at 2am on TikTok feel like we were made for her — or for her mom? The goal: both feel it. But the daughter has to feel it first.*

### The Mom

She arrives through her daughter. She doesn't need to understand every design choice — she just needs to trust the overall signal. Elevated. Intentional. Not trying too hard. The kind of brand her daughter would approve of.

**What she needs from us:** Confidence that this is the right level. That it's thoughtful without being cold. That it's worth it.

> *Design test: Would the mom say "I don't fully get it but I know she'll love it" — and feel good about that?*

# The Five Principles

## 1. Presence, Not Memory

Small Plates does not preserve memories. It extends presence.

| Memory Looks Like | Presence Looks Like |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Sepia tones, faded colors | Warm but vibrant colors |
| Static, frozen moments | Movement, mid-action |
| Precious, untouchable objects | Used, lived-in objects |
| Framed on a wall | Open on a counter |
| "Remember when" | "Still here" |

**Test:** "Does this feel like it's happening now, or like it happened in the past?"

---

## 2. Warmth as Presence

Warmth is how presence **feels**. Cold creates distance. Warmth creates proximity.

| Cold (Distance) | Warm (Presence) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Blue undertones | Golden undertones |
| Stark white | Cream, warm white |
| Hard edges | Soft textures |
| Clinical precision | Human imperfection |
| Minimalism as emptiness | Minimalism as breathing room |

**Test:** "Does this feel warm — like someone is here with me? Or does it feel cold — like I'm alone?"

---

## 3. The Collective Made Visible

Not one voice. A chorus. Not one recipe. Many.

| Singular | Collective |
|----------|------------|
| One hero image | Many hands, many names |
| Single voice | Chorus of voices |
| The couple alone | The couple surrounded |
| One recipe featured | Many recipes visible |
| Anonymous object | Named contributions |

**Test:** "Does this feel like many people, or just one? Does it feel like a collective, or isolated?"

---

## 4. Editorial, Not Ceremonial

Small Plates should feel like a high-end publication, not a wedding product.

| Ceremonial | Editorial |
|------------|-----------|
| Precious, framed | Functional, used |
| Special occasion | Everyday life |
| Displayed on a shelf | Integrated in the kitchen |
| Wedding industry aesthetic | Publishing industry aesthetic |
| Keepsake | Kitchen book |

**Test:** "Does this feel like it belongs in a lifestyle publication, or in a wedding planning guide?"

---

## 5. Irreverence Earns Emotion

*This principle is new in Version 2.0. It captures the Gen Z alignment.*

The wedding industry treats everything as sacred. Small Plates doesn't.

We can find the rehearsal dinner slightly absurd and the vows completely devastating — on the same day. That tension is not a contradiction. It's honesty. And honesty is what earns trust with people who have been marketed to their entire lives.

**Irreverence is not disrespect.** It's the acknowledgment that weddings are simultaneously one of the most meaningful things humans do and also a little bit ridiculous in their scale and ritual. Gen Z already knows this. When we say it first, we become the only wedding brand they trust.

| Reverence (everyone else) | Irreverence + Emotion (Small Plates) |
|---------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| "Your special day" | "One day. A lot of money. Worth it if the people are right." |
| Every detail is magical | Some details are absurd. The people aren't. |
| Leads with emotion | Earns emotion through observation |
| Treats the wedding as the point | Treats the people as the point |
| Would make the mom of the bride cry | Makes her laugh first, then cry |

**Test:** "Does this say something the wedding industry would never say — but that every bride immediately recognizes as true?"

# The Emotional Sequence

Smart funny. Not joke funny. Not sarcasm. Not snark.

The kind of humor that comes from observing something so precisely that people laugh because it's true. And then — once the laugh opens the door — the feeling walks through it.

**The sequence, always in this order:**

1. **"Ha"** — the smart observation. The thing everyone knows but nobody's naming.
2. **"...oh"** — the universal truth underneath. The recognition.
3. **"...damn"** — the emotion you didn't see coming.

Never skip to the emotion — that's a greeting card.
Never stay at the joke — that's a meme account.
The power is in the turn.

**How it works:**

> Your grandmother can't be in your kitchen every day.
> *(smile — you love her, but... yeah)*
>
> Her recipe can.
> *(...damn)*

> Another registry. Another stand mixer.
> *(ha — you've bought one of these)*
>
> The stand mixer ends up in the cabinet.
> *(oh — that's me)*
>
> Her grandmother's recipe doesn't.
> *(...damn)*

> Your future mother-in-law will contribute a recipe.
> That's probably the first real conversation you'll have with her.
> *(laugh — because it's exactly true — and then you realize that's actually kind of beautiful)*

**The rule:** The humor comes from precision, not from trying. If you have to explain why it's funny, it's not. If it doesn't lead somewhere real, cut it. The playful surface is what earns us the right to hit hard underneath.

**Test:** "Does this make someone laugh first and feel something second — in that order?"

# The Image Types

## Type 1 — The Uncomfortable Truth

**Purpose:** Stop the scroll. Make someone recognize themselves.
**Format:** Text only. No image. The white space is the message.
**Primary platform:** Instagram feed, TikTok text overlay

Examples:
```
Wedding gifts are forgotten in three months.
You know it too.
```
```
Another registry.
Another thing nobody asked for.
```
```
The stand mixer is in the cabinet.
The recipe is on the counter.
```

Rules:
- Warm White background. Soft Charcoal text. Nothing else.
- One thought. Never two.
- The second line does the emotional work. The first line earns it.

---

## Type 2 — Real, No Apologies

**Purpose:** Show the product in its real life, not its aspirational life.
**Format:** Documentary photography. No staging. No studio.
**Primary platform:** Instagram feed, TikTok B-roll

Images we want:
- The book open on a kitchen counter. Next to it: a half-empty glass of wine, a half-cut onion, a phone with notifications. Real life interrupted by a recipe.
- An iPhone screen showing a WhatsApp message: *"sending my mom's recipe now"*. Nothing else needed.
- A page of the book in close-up. The contributor's name visible. Typography fills the frame.
- Hands with flour or olive oil on them, turning pages.
- The book on the floor of a small apartment kitchen. NYC energy.
- The book open at a Sunday lunch table. Dishes around it. Someone just grabbed it mid-meal.

Rules:
- Don't clean the kitchen before shooting.
- Don't arrange the props.
- Don't look for perfect light — window light, lamp light, whatever's there.
- If there's a weird magnet on the fridge, it stays.
- If the counter is slightly messy, that's the shot.

---

## Type 3 — The Impact Moment

**Purpose:** Show emotion without announcing it.
**Format:** A real moment. No poses. No faces smiling at camera.
**Primary platform:** Instagram feed, TikTok payoff

Images we want:
- Someone reading the book and stopping on a page. Just the body's reaction — no face needed.
- The book open to a recipe from someone who's no longer here. Just the page. Nothing else.
- Two people in a kitchen at night. One reads, one cooks. The book is between them.
- The book on a shelf between actual cookbooks. It belongs there. It's earned its place.

Rules:
- No catalog smiles.
- No couples posing with the book for the camera.
- No unboxing aesthetic — no tissue paper, no ribbon, no reveal face.
- No exaggerated reactions. The quieter the moment, the harder it lands.

---

## Type 4 — The Orchestrator's View

**Purpose:** Show the MOH's hidden role. The person who made this happen.
**Format:** Candid, documentary. The private moment before the public one.
**Primary platform:** TikTok (POV format), Instagram stories

Images / video we want:
- A phone screen showing the dashboard — recipe count, progress bar. The MOH checking in from her couch at 11pm.
- The MOH holding the finished book before the bride sees it. That private beat of satisfaction.
- A notification: "3 new recipes submitted." Life continuing normally around it.
- The MOH at the wedding, watching the bride open the book. Not the bride's reaction — the MOH's face.
- A text thread: someone finally submitted their recipe. The relief.

Rules:
- Show the MOH as the person who pulled this off — not the person who struggled through it.
- Never show her chasing people or looking stressed.
- The dashboard should look clean, competent, functional. Not decorative.
- These images work as social proof for other MOHs watching: *"that could be me."*

---

## Type 5 — The Gen Z Direct Address

**Purpose:** Talk to the MOH and the bride the way they actually talk to each other.
**Format:** Talking head or text-native video. No production required.
**Primary platform:** TikTok (primary), Instagram Reels

*This image type is new in Version 2.0.*

The format is native TikTok. iPhone. Good natural light. One person — Ana — saying something true, directly. No hook that takes three seconds to get to the point. No outro. Starts in the middle. Ends when it's done.

Content angles:
- "Nobody talks about what happens to wedding gifts five years later."
- "POV: you're the MOH and you just found the thing."
- "The wedding registry is designed for guests, not for the couple."
- Things that are true about weddings that nobody in the wedding industry will say.

Rules:
- Ana speaks like Margot thinks — dry, precise, a beat of humor before the real thing.
- No script energy. Conversational, but intentional.
- Never name the product in the first 10 seconds.
- Never explain more than necessary. Stop when the point is made.
- The product is the conclusion, not the subject.

# Text as Image

Small Plates has a weapon that purely visual brands don't: **copy that lands.**

We use text as image — consistently, deliberately, without apology.

**Format A — Fill the whole frame:**
```
Your grandmother can't be
in your kitchen every day.

Her recipe can.
```
Large typography. Warm White. Generous space. Nothing else.

**Format B — Brutal contrast:**
Soft Charcoal background. Warm White text. One line.
```
It's not a recipe book.
It's the people who showed up.
```

**Format C — Real screenshot:**
The WhatsApp message from someone sending their recipe.
The text thread where the MOH is following up with the group.
Unedited. Unembellished. That is the content.

**Format D — The Dry Observation:** *(New in Version 2.0)*
Short, slightly irreverent, lands before you see it coming.
```
Another registry.
Another stand mixer.
Another cabinet it'll never leave.
```
Or:
```
The wedding lasts one day.
Everyone talks about the wedding.
```
Warm White or Soft Charcoal. Tight leading. No decoration.

# Photography Principles

## The Four Laws

**Law One: Anonymity Creates Universality**

Strong preference: don't show faces. A face belongs to someone specific. A gesture belongs to everyone. When viewers cannot see who the subjects are, they become the subjects. The bride becomes *every* bride. The MOH becomes *every* MOH.

Exception: raw documentary moments where the face adds emotional truth — but never looking at camera, never performing emotion.

**Law Two: Objects Tell Stories**

Hands matter. What hands hold matters more. A cast iron skillet says "we will build something together." A worn cookbook says "this has been used." Every object in frame must earn its presence. Objects that look new, unused, or staged undermine the entire message.

**Law Three: Tension Creates Interest**

Tension is the distance between expectation and reality. The book on the kitchen floor instead of on a shelf — tension. A recipe page visible next to a half-empty glass of wine — tension. Something elevated in a completely ordinary setting — tension. Without tension, photographs are forgettable.

**Law Four: The Invisible Hand**

Occasionally, reveal the MOH's hidden orchestration. The person who set this in motion, who sent the reminder texts, who checked the dashboard, who held the book before anyone else saw it — she's usually invisible in the final product.

When we show her moment of quiet satisfaction, we validate every MOH watching. She sees herself. She thinks: *I want to be that person.*

# What We Never Do Visually

| Never | Because |
|-------|---------|
| Show flowers, arches, or ceremony decor | We're not a wedding brand. We're a presence brand. |
| Show a bride posing with the book | Turns the product into a prop in someone else's performance |
| Use studio lighting | Makes everything look like an ad |
| Arrange props before shooting | The arrangement is the lie |
| Show the product new and unwrapped | Used is better. Stained is better. Real is always better. |
| Use blush pink, sage green, or rose gold | The industry's palette. We have our own. |
| Show exaggerated reactions | Earned emotion is quiet. Performed emotion is embarrassing. |
| Use stock photography | It always looks like stock photography. |
| Center the book in every frame | It should feel like it belongs, not like it's on display |
