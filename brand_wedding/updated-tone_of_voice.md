# Tone of Voice Guide
## Small Plates — Version 4.0

# How We Sound

---

## The Voice

Small Plates sounds like the friend who finally says the thing.

The thing about wedding gifts nobody admits.
The thing about keepsakes that end up in closets.
The thing about how most registries are designed for the guest's comfort, not the couple's life.

She says it without drama.
Without needing to make a moment of it.
Just — says it. Because it's true. And it opens the door to something real.

Then, once the door is open, she walks through it.

That's us. The observation first. The emotion earned.

---

## The Formula

This is the rule that governs everything:

> **Cool on the outside. Emotional on the inside.**

Not the reverse. Never the reverse.

The surface is confident, stylish, slightly irreverent.
The core is deeply human, genuinely caring, emotionally real.

Think of it like the book itself:
- **The cover:** Minimal. Designed. Something you want on display.
- **The pages:** Handwritten notes. Family recipes. Names that make you stop.

The voice works the same way.

We don't lead with emotion. We earn it.
We don't beg for attention. We deserve it.
We don't explain why this matters. We show it.

And sometimes — we're allowed to find the wedding industry slightly ridiculous
before we show why this one thing in it actually matters.

## The Five Principles

---

### 1. Warm, With Edge

We care. Genuinely.
But we're not soft about it.

Warmth doesn't mean gentle.
It means present. Real. Human.

We're the friend who shows up with food when you're sad —
and tells you the truth *before* you make the mistake, not after.

Warmth doesn't protect you from the truth.
It's what makes the truth land without damage.

**Warm with edge** = Love that doesn't coddle.

We sound like:
- Someone who actually knows you
- A friend, not a brand
- Someone who names the thing nobody's naming
- Honest, even when it's easier not to be
- The person who says "honestly? skip it" — and you know they're right

We don't sound like:
- A greeting card
- Customer service
- Someone trying too hard to be liked
- Someone who softens until the point disappears

---

### 2. Direct, Not Blunt

We say what we mean.
First sentence. No warmup.

But direct doesn't mean harsh.
It means we respect your time.
We get to the point because the point is worth getting to.

**Direct, not blunt** = Clarity with care.

We sound like:
- Someone confident in what they're saying
- Efficient without being cold
- Clear, even about emotional things

We don't sound like:
- A press release
- A legal disclaimer
- Someone who uses ten words when three would do

---

### 3. Effortless, Not Lazy

We make it look easy.
That's the skill.

The writing flows. The rhythm feels natural.
But underneath, every word is chosen.

**Effortless, not lazy** = The craft is invisible.

We sound like:
- Someone who's naturally good with words
- Cool without trying
- Smart without showing off

We don't sound like:
- Overly casual or sloppy
- Trying to be "relatable"
- Like we didn't care enough to edit

---

### 4. Moving, Not Pushing

We create momentum.
We make people want to act.

But we never pressure. Never guilt. Never create fake urgency.

The energy comes from excitement, not anxiety.

**Moving, not pushing** = Forward motion without force.

We sound like:
- Someone with a good idea who wants to share it
- A brand that makes you want to move
- Energizing without being exhausting

We don't sound like:
- A countdown timer
- A sales pitch
- Someone who needs you to buy this right now

---

### 5. Say the Quiet Part

The wedding industry is afraid of the truth.
We're not.

Not to provoke. Not to stand out.
But because we have enough confidence to be honest
when everyone else is being careful.

Most gifts end up in a drawer — and everyone knows it.
Most wedding keepsakes get one look and disappear — and everyone knows it.
The stand mixer is in the cabinet — and the couple who registered for it knows it.

We say these things.
Not to be contrarian.
But because naming the real thing is the only way to offer something real instead.

**In Version 4.0, this principle has an edge it didn't have before.**

We're now willing to be mildly irreverent about the wedding industry itself —
not the couples, not the love, not the meaning.
But the industry, the ritual, the $800 centerpieces and the 8-minute best man speeches.

That irreverence is what earns Gen Z's trust.
They already find it slightly absurd.
When we say it first, we become the only wedding brand they believe.

**Say the quiet part** = Name what everyone's thinking and nobody's saying.
Including the slightly absurd parts.

We sound like:
- A brand that doesn't need to protect the industry it's in
- Someone who finally says what's in the room
- Honest without being harsh. Wry without being mean.
- The person who finds the rehearsal dinner a bit much
  *and* cries during the vows
  *in that order, always in that order*

We don't sound like:
- A brand playing it safe to avoid offending anyone
- Provocative for the sake of attention
- Someone who softens the truth until it disappears
- Edgy without substance

## The Emotional Sequence

Every piece of communication follows this order. No exceptions.

1. **"Ha"** — The smart observation. The thing everyone knows but nobody's naming.
2. **"...oh"** — The universal truth underneath. The recognition.
3. **"...damn"** — The emotion you didn't see coming.

Never skip to the emotion — that's a greeting card.
Never stay at the joke — that's a meme account.
The power is in the turn.

**Examples:**

> Another registry. Another stand mixer.
> *(ha — you've bought one of these)*
>
> It's in the cabinet.
> *(...oh)*
>
> Her grandmother's recipe isn't.
> *(...damn)*

> A cookbook sounds boring.
> *(ha — true, it does)*
>
> Until you read what they wrote.
> *(...damn)*

> Your future mother-in-law will contribute a recipe.
> That's probably the first real conversation you'll have with her.
> *(laugh — and then realize that's actually kind of beautiful)*

**The rule:** The humor comes from precision, not from trying.
If you have to explain why it's funny, it's not.
If it doesn't lead somewhere real, cut it.

## Tone by Audience

The voice stays the same.
The tone adjusts based on who we're talking to and what they need to feel.

---

### The Bride — As Gift Receiver

**Who she is in 2026:**
She's Gen Z or younger millennial. She's seen every wedding on Instagram and TikTok.
She's slightly allergic to anything that feels performed.
She wants her wedding to feel like her people, not like a production.

**What she fears:**
Looking like she cared too much about the wrong things.
A gift that makes guests think "sweet" instead of "wow."

**What she needs to hear:**
"This is cool. This is real. This belongs in your kitchen, not a closet."

**How we sound to her:**
Dry. Confident. A little wry about weddings generally.
Like a brand that gets that she's more than a bride.

**Example — Social:**
> Your registry is full of things you'll return.
>
> This isn't one of them.
> It's made by the people who showed up.
> And it lives in your kitchen.
> Where you'll actually see it.

---

### The MOH — The Protagonist

*In Version 4.0, the MOH gets her own full section. She is the most important person in our universe.*

**Who she is:**
26-32. Organized, stretched thin, quietly proud.
She's already doing 14 things for this wedding.
She is not looking for one more task — she's looking for one thing that makes all the other tasks worth it.

She's also the person in the group who finds things nobody else finds.
The restaurant before it's on every list.
The gift before it's everywhere.
That identity matters to her. Deeply.

**Her three fears:**
- **Ineptitude:** "What if this falls apart and it's my fault?"
- **Subjugation:** "What if I do all the work and nobody notices?"
- **The wrong vibe:** "What if this feels like something her mom organized?"

**What she actually needs:**
To feel like a protagonist, not an intermediary.
To feel like Small Plates represents *her* — her taste, her effort, her decision.
To be able to say (without saying it out loud): *I found this. I made this happen.*

**How we sound to her:**
COO to COO. No hand-holding. No reassurance she didn't ask for.
Equal parts respect and excitement.
Like we know what she's carrying and we're the one thing that actually carries itself.

**The critical insight for tone:**
If the brand feels like something the mom would organize, the MOH won't organize it.
She has to feel like she *discovered* this. Like it's *hers* to give.

**Example — TikTok hook:**
> Nobody talks about what the MOH actually goes through.
>
> You're coordinating 12 people, a group chat that won't stop,
> and somehow also supposed to find the gift.
>
> Here's the one that does all of this for you.

**Example — Instagram:**
> You set it up.
> They submit their recipes.
> You hand her the book.
>
> That's it.
> That's the whole job.
>
> [Start →]

**Example — Email subject line:**
> You're about to be the MVP of this wedding.

### TikTok — Native Video

*New in Version 4.0. This register didn't exist in previous versions.*

TikTok has its own rules. We follow them.

**The TikTok register:**
- Starts in the middle. No intro. No "hey guys."
- First sentence is the whole argument in miniature.
- Conversational but intentional — sounds off-the-cuff, was absolutely not.
- Names the uncomfortable thing immediately.
- Never explains more than necessary.
- Product is the conclusion, not the subject.
- Ends when the point is made. Not before, not after.

**What Ana sounds like on TikTok:**
Dry. Observational. Like someone who's been to too many weddings and finally has something useful to say about all of them. Not a salesperson. A witness.

**Hook formulas that work:**

> "Nobody talks about what happens to wedding gifts five years later."

> "POV: you're the MOH and you just found the thing."

> "The wedding registry is designed for guests, not for the couple. Here's what I mean."

> "I looked at what brides actually remember about their gifts. The answer is not what you think."

**Hook formulas that don't work:**

> "Hi! Today I want to talk about something really exciting—"
*(Too much warmup. We lost them.)*

> "Small Plates is the perfect wedding gift because—"
*(Product first = ad. Skip.)*

> "Have you ever thought about how wedding gifts..."
*(Slow. Passive. Gone.)*

**The TikTok test:**
Read the first sentence out loud. Would you keep watching?
If you'd keep watching — ship it.
If you'd scroll — rewrite the first sentence.

## Do's and Don'ts

### Do

- **Start strong.** First sentence earns the second.
- **Name the uncomfortable thing.** Say it before they think it. That's what builds trust.
- **End with momentum.** Last line makes them want to act or feel something.
- **Use "you."** We're talking to someone, not at them.
- **Be specific.** "Your mom's pasta" > "a family recipe."
- **Make it easy.** Reduce friction in every word.
- **Sound certain.** "This is good" > "We think you'll like this."
- **Mix short and long.** Rhythm matters.
- **Read it out loud.** If it sounds like a brand, rewrite it.
- **Let the MOH feel like a protagonist.** She made this happen. Say so.
- **Be willing to be wry about weddings.** Not the love. The industry.

### Don't

- **Don't hedge.** No "maybe," "perhaps," "we think."
- **Don't over-explain.** Trust the reader.
- **Don't use exclamation points.** Confidence doesn't shout.
- **Don't start with "We're excited..."** We're not a press release.
- **Don't be passive.** Even emotional moments move forward.
- **Don't guilt or pressure.** Energy yes, anxiety no.
- **Don't use emojis.** Exception: Stories, sparingly.
- **Don't sacrifice clarity for style.** Cool but confusing = rewrite.
- **Don't soften until there's nothing left.** Edge is not cruelty. It's honesty.
- **Don't reassure when you should respect.** The MOH doesn't need "don't worry." She needs "it's handled."
- **Don't try to be Gen Z.** Just be honest. That's what Gen Z responds to.

## The Gut Checks

*Version 4.0 has two gut checks. Both must pass.*

**Gut Check 1 — The Margot Test:**
> "Would our 28-year-old Brooklyn Creative Director,
> glass of natural wine in hand at a dinner that got real at 11pm,
> say this — or share it without explanation?"

If she'd roll her eyes → rewrite.
If she'd say it with a knowing smile → ship it.
If it would make everyone at the table go quiet for a second → even better.

**Gut Check 2 — The MOH Test:**
> "Would the MOH — 26, organized, the person who finds things before everyone else —
> feel like this brand was made for her?
> Or would she feel like it was made for her mom?"

If it feels like something her mom would organize → rewrite.
If it feels like something *she* discovered → ship it.
If her mom would find it through her and also love it → that's the goal.

**The relationship between the two tests:**
Margot is the brand. The MOH sees herself in Margot.
If Margot would approve, the MOH feels represented.
If the mom trusts Margot, she trusts the brand.

One voice. Every audience finds herself in it.
