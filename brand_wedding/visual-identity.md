# Visual Identity Brief
## Small Plates & Co. — Version 1.0

---

# The Vision

Small Plates should look like it **feels**.

And it feels like:
- Champagne on a terrace at night
- Jazz playing low in the background
- A late dinner with people you actually like
- Something you want in your home for a long time

The visual identity must communicate:

> **Cool on the outside. Emotional on the inside.**

Sophisticated but alive.
Elegant but warm.
Editorial but human.
Premium but not pretentious.

---

# The Woman We're Designing For

Every visual decision should pass through this filter:

> **Would a 28-year-old woman with New York energy, confident and stylish, want this in her kitchen? Would she proudly show it to her friends?**

She wears:
- A long dress, minimal but intentional
- Neutral tones with one bold detail
- Natural makeup that still feels polished

She is not:
- Basic
- Cheesy
- Overly traditional
- Pinterest-predictable

She is:
- Effortlessly cool
- Genuinely warm
- Quietly confident

The visual identity should feel like **her**.

---

The wedding industry is drowning in:
- Blush pink
- Sage green
- Rose gold
- Safe neutrals

Small Plates needs to stand apart.

The palette is built on **warmth with sophistication**—colors that feel like golden hour, candlelight dinners, honey in the sun. Not cold. Not corporate. Not predictable.

## Color Usage Rules

### Do

- Use Honey sparingly—it's the spice, not the main dish
- Let Warm White breathe—generous whitespace is part of the identity
- Use Soft Charcoal for most text—it's softer than pure black
- Let photography bring additional color naturally

### Don't

- Don't use Honey for large background areas (overwhelming)
- Don't use pure black (#000000)—always Soft Charcoal or Deep Charcoal
- Don't use pure white (#FFFFFF)—always Warm White or Cream
- Don't introduce pinks, blues, or purples—they break the warmth

# Typography

## Philosophy

The typography must balance:
- **Editorial sophistication** (we're a book company)
- **Modern clarity** (we're a tech-enabled product)
- **Human warmth** (we're about people and connection)

---

## Primary Type System

### Serif — Minion Pro

**Usage:** Headlines, titles, emotional moments, the book itself

**Why it works:**
- Classic editorial feel
- Highly readable at various sizes
- Sophisticated without being stuffy
- Works beautifully in print (important for the book)

**Weights to use:**
- Regular (body in book)
- Semibold (headlines)
- Italic (emphasis, quotes, special moments)

---

### Sans-Serif — Inter

**Usage:** Body text (digital), UI elements, functional copy, captions

**Why it works:**
- Extremely legible on screens
- Modern but not cold
- Pairs perfectly with Minion Pro
- Versatile weight range

**Weights to use:**
- Regular (body)
- Medium (emphasis)
- Semibold (buttons, labels)
- Bold (sparingly, for strong emphasis)

---

### Script — To Be Selected

**Usage:** "& Co." in logo, special moments, signature feel

**Direction:** Elegant but not overly formal. Should feel like beautiful handwriting from someone with good taste—not calligraphy, not childish.

**Characteristics to look for:**
- Fluid but readable
- Slightly irregular (human, not mechanical)
- Works at small sizes
- Feels personal, not performative

**Reference styles:**
- Modern calligraphy (not traditional)
- Fashion editorial scripts
- High-end restaurant menus

**Candidates to explore:**
- Burgues Script
- Carolyna Pro Black
- Wishes Script
- Playlist Script
- Andora Modern Script

**Note:** The script is an accent, not a workhorse. Use sparingly for maximum impact.

---

## Type Hierarchy

| Level | Font | Weight | Size (Desktop) | Size (Mobile) | Use Case |
|-------|------|--------|----------------|---------------|----------|
| H1 | Minion Pro | Semibold | 48-64px | 32-40px | Page heroes, major headlines |
| H2 | Minion Pro | Semibold | 36-42px | 28-32px | Section headers |
| H3 | Minion Pro | Regular | 24-30px | 20-24px | Subsection headers |
| Body | Inter | Regular | 16-18px | 16px | Paragraphs, descriptions |
| Small | Inter | Regular | 14px | 14px | Captions, labels |
| Button | Inter | Semibold | 14-16px | 14px | CTAs, buttons |
| Accent | Script | — | Variable | Variable | "& Co.", special moments |

---

## Typography Rules

### Do

- Mix Minion Pro and Inter intentionally—serif for emotion, sans for function
- Use generous line height (1.5-1.7 for body text)
- Let headlines breathe with ample spacing
- Use italic Minion Pro for quotes and emphasis

### Don't

- Don't use all caps for long text (okay for short labels)
- Don't use script for body text—ever
- Don't mix more than these three typefaces
- Don't use thin/light weights—they feel weak

# Logo Direction

## Current State

```
Small
plates
& Co.
```

**What works:**
- Clean, minimal
- Good type contrast (Minion Pro + Inter bold)

**What needs work:**
- "& Co." feels like an afterthought
- The double meaning isn't visible
- Lacks warmth and personality
- Doesn't differentiate from generic brands

---

## The Strategic Opportunity

"& Co." is not just a name suffix—it's the **heart of the concept**.

Small Plates = the food, the recipes, the book
& Company = the people, the presence, the connection

The logo should make this duality **visible**.

---

## Direction 1: Script "& Co."

```
Small Plates
      & Co.
```

- "Small Plates" in Minion Pro (as current)
- "& Co." in elegant script
- Script adds warmth, humanity, personal touch
- Connects to wedding aesthetic without being obvious
- The ampersand becomes a visual signature

**Variations to explore:**
- Script slightly overlapping the serif text
- Script in Honey color
- Script underlined with a subtle stroke

---

## Direction 2: Emphasized Ampersand

```
Small Plates
    & Co.
```

- The "&" becomes a design element
- Larger, bolder, possibly in Honey
- Could be a standalone icon/mark
- "Co." in Inter or script

**The "&" as brand mark:**
- Use alone as favicon, social avatar, app icon
- The symbol of connection
- Simple, ownable, memorable

---

## Direction 3: Stacked with Intention

```
SMALL PLATES
& Company
```

- "SMALL PLATES" in spaced caps (Minion Pro)
- "& Company" in script, written out fully
- More formal, more editorial
- "Company" spelled out emphasizes the double meaning

---

## Direction 4: Integrated Script

```
Small Plates & Co.
```

- All on one line
- "Small Plates" in Minion Pro
- "&" as transitional element (could be decorative)
- "Co." in script, connecting fluidly

# Photography Style

## Philosophy

Small Plates photography should feel like **a really talented friend is documenting real moments**—not a commercial photographer staging scenes.

The goal is:
- Authentic over perfect
- Warm over cool
- Dynamic over static
- Human over product

---

## The Feeling

Every photo should feel like:
- A stolen moment at a great dinner party
- The warmth of golden hour
- Friends who actually like each other
- Real joy, not performed joy

---

## Technical Direction

### Lighting

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Natural light, especially golden hour | Harsh flash or studio lighting |
| Soft, directional light | Flat, even lighting |
| Warm color temperature | Cool or blue tones |
| Dappled light, shadows that add depth | Overexposed, blown-out highlights |
| Candlelight, string lights for evening | Clinical, corporate lighting |

---

### Composition

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Movement, action, mid-moment | Static, posed, frozen |
| Slightly imperfect framing | Over-composed, too perfect |
| Depth of field (subject focus, soft background) | Everything in sharp focus |
| Interesting angles (overhead, close-up details) | Straight-on, predictable |
| Negative space that breathes | Cluttered, busy frames |

---

### Subject Matter

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| People interacting with food | Food alone, isolated |
| Hands cooking, serving, sharing | Untouched, pristine plates |
| Real expressions, laughter, connection | Posed smiles, looking at camera |
| Groups of friends, families, couples | Solo individuals (usually) |
| Lived-in kitchens, real tables | Staged, sterile environments |
| The book being USED (stained, open, in action) | The book as a pristine object |

---

### Color in Photography

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Warm tones that match the palette | Cool, desaturated, moody |
| Natural colors of food and skin | Over-filtered or heavily edited |
| Honey/golden accents when possible | Neon, artificial colors |
| Earth tones, naturals, creams | Bright pinks, blues, purples |

---

## Photography Categories

### 1. Lifestyle / People

**Primary category.** This is what differentiates Small Plates.

- Friends around a table
- Couples cooking together
- Families sharing meals
- Hands passing dishes
- Candid laughter, real moments
- Wedding-adjacent without being "wedding photography"

**Energy level:** High. Dynamic. Alive.

---

### 2. Food

**Secondary.** Always in context, never isolated.

- Dishes being prepared or served
- Tables full of shared plates
- Ingredients in motion
- Imperfect, delicious, real food
- Never overly styled "food porn"

**Energy level:** Medium. Inviting. Approachable.

---

### 3. Product (The Book)

**Tertiary.** The book is the outcome, not the hero.

- The book in a real kitchen
- The book being cooked from (open, used)
- Hands turning pages
- Close-ups of handwritten notes, names
- Slightly worn, loved, lived-in

**Energy level:** Lower. Intimate. Emotional.

---

## Photography Don'ts — Absolute Rules

| Never | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Stock photography feeling | Kills authenticity |
| People looking at camera smiling | Feels staged, ad-like |
| Isolated product shots on white | Cold, e-commerce, not us |
| Perfectly styled food | Not real, not approachable |
| Cool/blue color grading | Contradicts brand warmth |
| Empty tables, empty kitchens | We're about people, not spaces |
| Overly "wedding" imagery (veils, rings, cakes) | We're adjacent to weddings, not a wedding brand |
| Sad, serious, somber moods | We're about joy and connection |

---

**Mood/Energy:**
- Bon Appétit (the human, fun era)

**Color/Warmth:**

# Part 1 — The Foundation

## The Brand Idea

**Vivid Presence**

The people who love you stay in your life—not as memories, but as presence.

Every color, every typeface, every photograph, every layout decision answers one question:

> **"Does this make the presence more vivid?"**

If the answer is yes, proceed.
If the answer is no, reconsider.

---

## What Vivid Presence Looks Like

Presence is:
- **Active**, not passive
- **Warm**, not cold
- **Used**, not preserved
- **Now**, not then
- **Many**, not one

---

## The Woman We're Designing For

Every visual decision passes through this filter:

> **Would a 28-year-old woman with New York energy, confident and stylish, want this in her kitchen? Would she proudly show it to her friends?**

She is not: basic, cheesy, overly traditional, Pinterest-predictable.
She is: effortlessly cool, genuinely warm, quietly confident.

The visual identity should feel like **her**.

---

# Part 2 — The Five Principles

```
BRAND IDEA: "Vivid Presence"
        │
        ▼
VISUAL IDENTITY PRINCIPLES
1. Presence, Not Memory
2. Warmth as Presence
3. The Collective Made Visible
4. Editorial, Not Ceremonial
5. Tension Between Cool and Warm
        │
        ▼
TACTICAL GUIDELINES
(Color, Typography, Photography, Applications)
        │
        ▼
EXECUTIONS
(Book, Website, Packaging, Social)
```

---

## Principle 1: Presence, Not Memory

Small Plates does not preserve memories. It extends presence.

| Memory Looks Like | Presence Looks Like |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Sepia tones, faded colors | Warm but vibrant colors |
| Static, frozen moments | Movement, mid-action |
| Precious, untouchable objects | Used, lived-in objects |
| Framed on a wall | Open on a counter |
| "Remember when" | "Still here" |

**Test:** "Does this feel like it's happening now, or like it happened in the past?"

---

## Principle 2: Warmth as Presence

Warmth is how presence **feels**. Cold creates distance. Warmth creates proximity.

| Cold (Distance) | Warm (Presence) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Blue undertones | Golden undertones |
| Stark white | Cream, warm white |
| Hard edges | Soft textures |
| Clinical precision | Human imperfection |
| Minimalism as emptiness | Minimalism as breathing room |

**Test:** "Does this feel warm—like someone is here with me? Or does it feel cold—like I'm alone?"

---

## Principle 3: The Collective Made Visible

Not one voice. A chorus. Not one recipe. Many.

The visual identity must express multiplicity—many people, many names, many contributions all held together in one object.

**Test:** "Does this feel like a collective, or isolated?"

---

## Principle 4: Editorial, Not Ceremonial

Small Plates should feel like a high-end publication, not a wedding product.

| Ceremonial | Editorial |
|------------|-----------|
| Precious, framed | Functional, used |
| Special occasion | Everyday life |
| Displayed on a shelf | Integrated in the kitchen |
| Wedding industry aesthetic | Publishing industry aesthetic |
| Keepsake | Kitchen book |

**Test:** "Does this feel like it belongs in a lifestyle publication, or in a wedding planning guide?"

---

## Principle 5: Tension Between Cool and Warm

The brand personality is "cool on the outside, emotional on the inside."

| Surface (Cool) | Core (Warm) |
|----------------|-------------|
| Minimal design | Rich content |
| Editorial restraint | Emotional depth |
| Sophisticated typography | Handwritten notes |
| Clean layout | Personal stories |
| Composed photography | Real human moments |

Without the cool surface, we're sentimental.
Without the warm core, we're cold.
The magic is in holding both.

**Test:** "Is there tension between the surface and the content? Does the cool exterior make the warm interior more powerful?"

# Part 5 — Photography & Creative Direction

## The Standard

The book lives on the counter with an oil stain on page 47
and a note in the margin that says *"add more garlic."*

That's the visual world of Small Plates.

Every image, every post, every creative decision answers one question:

> **"Would someone see this on Instagram and think: 'wait — that's a wedding recipe book?'"**

If the answer is **yes, and with surprise** — you're on track.
If the answer is **"obviously, it looks like a wedding gift"** — start over.

---

## References

### In

| Reference | Why |
|-----------|-----|
| **Liquid Death** | Sold water like it was heavy metal. Nobody expected it. Same product. Completely different world. |
| **Oatly (OOH)** | Giant text, self-referential, no product in the image. They trust the words to do the work. |
| **Billie** | Shows what the industry never showed. Not afraid of real. |
| **Vice / i-D editorial** | Direct, urban, no pretense of "beautiful lifestyle." Cool comes from honesty, not production. |
| **Documentary food** | Not food porn. Real food. Real kitchens. The actual chaos of cooking. |

### Out

| Out | Why |
|-----|-----|
| Kinfolk | Too soft. Too quiet. The aspirational version nobody aspires to anymore. |
| Ottolenghi | Precious. Untouchable. The book you display, not the one you use. |
| Cereal / Phaidon | Editorially correct but cold. No tension. No surprise. |
| Golden hour as default | Predictable. Every wedding brand lives there. |
| Beautiful wooden tables | The visual cliché of the premium gift. Makes us look like everyone else. |

---

## The Energy Shift

The book is the vehicle. Presence is the product.
Every image says: **these people are in your kitchen, even when they can't be.**

Not warm and aspirational. **Real and unexpected.**

| Out | In |
|-----|----|
| Magazine kitchen, spotless | Real apartment kitchen, with mess |
| Golden hour, soft | Kitchen light at night. The hour you actually cook. |
| Soft hands gently turning pages | Hands with flour on them, reading the book |
| The book as an object to admire | The book in the middle of cooking chaos |
| Lifestyle staging | Captured moment, not constructed |
| High production, low emotion | Minimum production, high emotion |

---

## The Three Image Types

### Type 1 — The Uncomfortable Truth
**Purpose:** Stop the scroll. Make someone recognize themselves.
**Format:** Text only. No image. The white space is the message.

Example:
```
Wedding gifts are forgotten in three months.
You know it too.
```
Warm White. Typography. Nothing else.

---

### Type 2 — Real, No Apologies
**Purpose:** Show the product in its real life, not its aspirational life.
**Format:** Documentary photography. No staging. No studio.

Images we want:
- The book open on a kitchen counter. Next to it: a half-empty glass of wine, a half-cut onion, a phone. Real life interrupted by a recipe.
- An iPhone screen with a WhatsApp message: *"my mom's recipe, sending it now."* Nothing else.
- A page of the book in close-up. The contributor's name visible. Large. Typography fills the frame.
- Hands with flour or oil on them, turning pages.
- The book on the floor of a small apartment kitchen. NYC energy. Not an accident — that's just how it lives.

Rules:
- Don't clean the kitchen before shooting.
- Don't arrange the props.
- Don't look for perfect light.
- If there's a weird magnet on the fridge in the background, it stays.

---

### Type 3 — The Impact Moment
**Purpose:** Show emotion without announcing it.
**Format:** A real moment. No poses. No faces smiling at camera.

Images we want:
- Someone reading the book and stopping on a page. Just the body's reaction — without seeing the face.
- The book open to a recipe from someone who's no longer here. Just the page. Nothing else needed.
- Two people in the kitchen at night. One reads, one cooks. The book is between them.

Rules:
- No catalog smiles.
- No couples posing with the book.
- No unboxing aesthetic.
- No exaggerated reactions.

## Text as Image

Small Plates has a weapon that purely visual brands don't: **copy that lands.**

We use text as image — consistently, deliberately, without apology.

**Format A — Fill the whole frame:**
```
Your grandmother can't be
in your kitchen every day.

Her recipe can.
```
Large typography. Warm White. Lots of space. Nothing else.

**Format B — Brutal contrast:**
Black background. White text. One line.
```
It's not a recipe book.
It's the people who showed up.
```

**Format C — Real screenshot:**
The WhatsApp message from someone sending their recipe.
Unedited. Unembellished. That is the content.

| Never | Why |
|-------|-----|
| Stock photography feeling | Kills authenticity |
| People looking at camera smiling | Feels staged, ad-like |

# Part 7 — The Tests

Before approving any visual asset, in this order:

**Test 1:**
> **"Would any other wedding gift brand have made this?"**
If yes — don't publish it.

**Test 2:**
> **"Would someone stop and say: 'wait, that's a recipe book? Didn't expect that.'"**
That surprise is the brand.

**Test 3 — The Margot Test:**
> **"Would our 28-year-old New York woman, champagne in hand on a rooftop, want this in her kitchen?"**
If she'd roll her eyes → rewrite.
If she'd say it with a knowing smile → ship it.

**Test 4 — The Presence Test:**
> **"Does this make the presence more vivid?"**
This is the ultimate test. Everything else serves this.

## Production Note

High production is the enemy.
High production screams "ad."
Minimum production screams "truth."

Ana Karen's iPhone.
A real kitchen.
Natural light or kitchen light.
No ring lights. No studio backgrounds. No purchased props.

The only investment that matters is **the real moment.**
If the moment is real, the image works.
If the moment is staged, no production fixes it.
