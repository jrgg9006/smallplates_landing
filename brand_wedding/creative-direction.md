# The Foundation

## The Brand Idea

**Vivid Presence**

The people who love you stay in your life — not as memories, but as presence.

Every color, every typeface, every photograph, every layout decision answers one question:

> **"Does this make the presence more vivid?"**

If the answer is yes, proceed.
If the answer is no, reconsider.

---

## The Key Insight

**The book is the vehicle. Presence is the product.**

Every image says: these people are in your kitchen, even when they can't be.

Not warm and aspirational. **Real and unexpected.**

---

## The Formula

> **Cool on the outside. Emotional on the inside.**

The surface is confident, stylish, effortless. The core is deeply human, genuinely caring, emotionally real.

We don't lead with emotion. We earn it. We don't beg for attention. We deserve it. We don't explain why this matters. We show it.

---

## The Woman We're Designing For

> **Would a 28-year-old woman with New York energy, confident and stylish, want this in her kitchen? Would she proudly show it to her friends?**

She is: effortlessly cool, genuinely warm, quietly confident.
She is not: basic, cheesy, overly traditional, Pinterest-predictable.

---

## The Woman We're Designing With

> **Would the MOH — organized, exhausted, holding everything together — feel respected by this? Would she feel like a collaborator, not a customer?**

She is: competent, stretched thin, quietly proud, detail-oriented.
She is not: someone who needs hand-holding, coddling, or cheerful reassurance.

Her experience should feel organized and professional — like a well-run project, not a craft activity. The dashboard should feel like a status report from a trusted partner. The communications should feel like working with someone who respects her time.

Design for her means: clean interfaces, clear progress indicators, operational language, zero decorative elements in functional contexts.

# The Five Principles

## 1. Presence, Not Memory

Small Plates does not preserve memories. It extends presence.

| Memory Looks Like | Presence Looks Like |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Sepia tones, faded colors | Warm but vibrant colors |
| Static, frozen moments | Movement, mid-action |
| Precious, untouchable objects | Used, lived-in objects |
| Framed on a wall | Open on a counter |
| "Remember when" | "Still here" |

**Test:** "Does this feel like it's happening now, or like it happened in the past?"

---

## 2. Warmth as Presence

Warmth is how presence **feels**. Cold creates distance. Warmth creates proximity.

| Cold (Distance) | Warm (Presence) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Blue undertones | Golden undertones |
| Stark white | Cream, warm white |
| Hard edges | Soft textures |
| Clinical precision | Human imperfection |
| Minimalism as emptiness | Minimalism as breathing room |

**Test:** "Does this feel warm — like someone is here with me? Or does it feel cold — like I'm alone?"

---

## 3. The Collective Made Visible

Not one voice. A chorus. Not one recipe. Many.

| Singular | Collective |
|----------|------------|
| One hero image | Many hands, many names |
| Single voice | Chorus of voices |
| The couple alone | The couple surrounded |
| One recipe featured | Many recipes visible |
| Anonymous object | Named contributions |

**Test:** "Does this feel like many people, or just one? Does it feel like a collective, or isolated?"

---

## 4. Editorial, Not Ceremonial

Small Plates should feel like a high-end publication, not a wedding product.

| Ceremonial | Editorial |
|------------|-----------|
| Precious, framed | Functional, used |
| Special occasion | Everyday life |
| Displayed on a shelf | Integrated in the kitchen |
| Wedding industry aesthetic | Publishing industry aesthetic |
| Keepsake | Kitchen book |

**Test:** "Does this feel like it belongs in a lifestyle publication, or in a wedding planning guide?"

# The Emotional Sequence

Smart funny. Not joke funny. Not sarcasm. Not snark.

The kind of humor that comes from observing something so precisely that people laugh because it's true. And then — once the laugh opens the door — the feeling walks through it.

**The sequence, always in this order:**

1. **"Ha"** — the smart observation. The thing everyone knows but nobody's naming.
2. **"...oh"** — the universal truth underneath. The recognition.
3. **"...damn"** — the emotion you didn't see coming.

Never skip to the emotion — that's a greeting card.
Never stay at the joke — that's a meme account.
The power is in the turn.

**How it works:**

> Your grandmother can't be in your kitchen every day.
> *(smile — you love her, but... yeah)*
>
> Her recipe can.
> *(...damn)*

> A cookbook sounds boring.
> *(ha — true, it does)*
>
> Until you read what they wrote.
> *(...oh)*

> Your future mother-in-law will contribute a recipe.
> That's probably the first real conversation you'll have with her.
> *(laugh — because it's exactly true — and then you realize that's actually kind of beautiful)*

**The rule:** The humor comes from precision, not from trying. If you have to explain why it's funny, it's not. If it doesn't lead somewhere real, cut it. The playful surface is what earns us the right to hit hard underneath.

**Test:** "Does this make someone laugh first and feel something second — in that order?"

# The Three Image Types

## Type 1 — The Uncomfortable Truth

**Purpose:** Stop the scroll. Make someone recognize themselves.
**Format:** Text only. No image. The white space is the message.

Example:
```
Wedding gifts are forgotten in three months.
You know it too.
```
Warm White. Typography. Nothing else.

---

## Type 2 — Real, No Apologies

**Purpose:** Show the product in its real life, not its aspirational life.
**Format:** Documentary photography. No staging. No studio.

Images we want:
- The book open on a kitchen counter. Next to it: a half-empty glass of wine, a half-cut onion, a phone. Real life interrupted by a recipe.
- An iPhone screen with a WhatsApp message: *"my mom's recipe, sending it now."* Nothing else.
- A page of the book in close-up. The contributor's name visible. Large. Typography fills the frame.
- Hands with flour or oil on them, turning pages.
- The book on the floor of a small apartment kitchen. NYC energy.

Rules:
- Don't clean the kitchen before shooting.
- Don't arrange the props.
- Don't look for perfect light.
- If there's a weird magnet on the fridge in the background, it stays.

---

## Type 3 — The Impact Moment

**Purpose:** Show emotion without announcing it.
**Format:** A real moment. No poses. No faces smiling at camera.

Images we want:
- Someone reading the book and stopping on a page. Just the body's reaction — without seeing the face.
- The book open to a recipe from someone who's no longer here. Just the page. Nothing else needed.
- Two people in the kitchen at night. One reads, one cooks. The book is between them.

Rules:
- No catalog smiles.
- No couples posing with the book.
- No unboxing aesthetic.
- No exaggerated reactions.

---

## Type 4 — The Orchestrator's View

**Purpose:** Show the organizer's hidden role. The person behind the gift.
**Format:** Candid, documentary. Focus on the private moment before the public one.

Images we want:
- A phone screen showing the dashboard — recipe count, progress bar. The organizer checking in from a coffee shop.
- The organizer holding the finished book before the bride sees it. The private moment of satisfaction.
- A notification on a phone: "3 new recipes submitted." Life continuing around it.
- The organizer at the wedding, watching the bride open the book. Not the bride's reaction — the organizer's.

Rules:
- Show the organizer as competent, not stressed.
- The dashboard should look clean and functional, not decorative.
- These images work as social proof for other organizers: "that could be me."
- Never show the organizer chasing people or looking overwhelmed.

# Text as Image

Small Plates has a weapon that purely visual brands don't: **copy that lands.**

We use text as image — consistently, deliberately, without apology.

**Format A — Fill the whole frame:**
```
Your grandmother can't be
in your kitchen every day.

Her recipe can.
```
Large typography. Warm White. Lots of space. Nothing else.

**Format B — Brutal contrast:**
Black background. White text. One line.
```
It's not a recipe book.
It's the people who showed up.
```

**Format C — Real screenshot:**
The WhatsApp message from someone sending their recipe.
Unedited. Unembellished. That is the content.

# Photography Principles

## The Three Laws

**Law One: Anonymity Creates Universality**

Strong preference: don't show faces. A face belongs to someone specific. A gesture belongs to everyone. When viewers cannot see who the subjects are, they become the subjects. The bride becomes *every* bride.

Exception: raw documentary moments where the face adds emotional truth. Never catalog smiles, never looking at camera.

**Law Two: Objects Tell Stories**

Hands matter. What hands hold matters more. A cast iron skillet says "we will build something together." A kettle says "our home will be warm." Every object in frame must earn its presence.

**Law Three: Tension Creates Interest**

Tension is the distance between expectation and reality. A bride in a suit instead of a gown — tension. Wedding attire in a garden, not a church — tension. Elegance holding a cast iron pan — tension. Without tension, photographs are forgettable.

**Law Four: The Invisible Hand**

Occasionally, reveal the organizer's hidden orchestration. The person who set this in motion, who invited the guests, who checked the dashboard — she's usually invisible in the final product. But sometimes, showing her moment of quiet satisfaction is more powerful than showing the bride's tears.

The invisible hand is the organizer who made it all happen without anyone knowing. When we reveal her, we validate every organizer watching.
