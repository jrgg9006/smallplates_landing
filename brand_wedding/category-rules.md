# Category Rules

*The wedding and cookbook industry rules Small Plates breaks, bends, and respects — and why.*

*This is a strategic positioning document. It maps where Small Plates stands against the two saturated categories it operates in: weddings and cookbooks. For operational visual language derived from this positioning, see `visual-principles.md`.*

---

## How this document works

We operate in two saturated categories. Both are visually and verbally predictable. Neither is disruptable by changing everything — that would be cosplay challenger, not strategy. Disruption is selective.

Rules are classified in four categories:

- **A1 — Manifesto rupture.** The rule whose breaking *is* Small Plates. Public declaration of what we are. Cult brands have 3–5. More than that dilutes focus.
- **A2 — Recognized rupture.** Genuine differentiator. Not the manifesto, but notable.
- **A2.5 — Intentional deviation.** We stay within the category's acceptable territory but choose variants at the edge, not the center.
- **B — Ignored.** Rules that exist but don't touch our product. No rupture because no contact.
- **C — Respected deliberately.** Rules we follow on purpose because breaking them would generate friction without strategic return.

---

## A1 — THE FIVE MANIFESTO RUPTURES

### A1.1 — We reject the entire verbal and narrative playbook of the wedding industry

The industry sells *"your special day / your perfect moment / forever starts here."* We don't. We use the opposite structure: *"everyone leaves / who stays / the afterward."* We refuse the banned vocabulary (*cherish, treasure, memories, special, unique, perfect, amazing, curated, journey, loved ones, celebrate*). We elevate the 80 guests as co-authors and refuse the exclusive protagonism of the couple. This is our most complete and unambiguous rupture.

### A1.2 — The buyer is the organizer, not the couple

Every wedding DTC brand assumes the couple buys their own wedding and guests buy registry gifts. We reject that model. Our buyer is the organizer (behaviorally defined, not demographically — MOH, sister, mom, aunt, friend, whoever has the proactive character). The couple receives. Product-level segmentation stops at the organizer.

### A1.3 — The gift is collective, not individual

The registry model is individual-guest-to-couple. Each guest picks a thing, sends a thing, checks a box. We invert this: 80 guests co-construct a single object. The product is a collaborative artifact, not an accumulation of individual gifts.

### A1.4 — Value activates in the afterward, even when the object is delivered during

Most wedding industry products consume their value *on the day* (flowers, catering, music, cake) or preserve static value (photo books, guestbooks — stored, not lived). Small Plates is typically delivered in the wedding timeframe — often at the bridal shower or a pre-wedding dinner, though this varies — but the object is built for the years after. We're not *for* the wedding. We're for the Wednesday.

### A1.5 — The only wedding gift that activates socially when opened

Every other wedding gift is solitary in the moment of unwrapping. Small Plates is collaborative in that moment. In production we've observed: when the couple receives the book in front of people, guests crowd around. They look for their own recipe. They discover who else contributed. The book passes from hand to hand. This behavior is structural to the design and is our viral coefficient. It's not a side effect. It's a product feature.

## A2 — RECOGNIZED RUPTURES

Real differentiators. Smaller than manifesto, important to document so they don't drift.

### A2.1 — We don't show the couple as visual protagonists

The industry's most basic rule is "couple front and center." We break it. Two functional exceptions only: (a) the guest recipe collection link (for identification: "this is for Ana & José, not anyone else") and (b) one image inside the book (personalization). In all marketing and brand construction, the couple is absent. The 80 guests are present through their handwriting, recipes, and contributions.

### A2.2 — We reject the mom-as-validator decision model

The industry assumes a "bride + mom" duo makes taste decisions. We reject this model entirely. Our audience is a single behavioral character (the organizer). If the mom happens to be the organizer, fine. But she's not a secondary validator of the bride. This is structural and already canonized in `foundation.md` and `organizer.md`.

### A2.3 — The book in use, not the book on display

Competitors photograph photobooks pristine, styled, aspirational — objects to preserve. We photograph the book in use: stained, open, on kitchen counters, next to flour, next to last night's half-finished glass of wine. This is a recognized rupture. We note: the photography is more expensive and harder to produce than styled shots, and the reward is not massive recognition — but coherence with the brand is non-negotiable.

---

## A2.5 — INTENTIONAL DEVIATIONS

We stay in the category's territory but pick variants at the edge.

### A2.5.1 — Typography: editorial sobriety, not wedding playbook

The wedding industry defaults to Playfair + script calligraphy. We use Minion Pro (editorial serif, book-world) + Inter (modern sans-serif, tech/editorial-world). No calligraphic script. The "& Co." in logo is Minion italic, not a third typeface. The system signals "editorial / product," not "wedding stationery." The deviation is deliberate but contained — enough to mark "not industry standard" without generating confusion.

### A2.5.2 — Photography of humans: real, not falsely spontaneous

The industry's docu-editorial style pretends to be candid but is orchestrated. Golden hour, composed, staged. We reject this. When humans appear in our photography (outside the couple, who don't appear), they're in real domestic contexts — restaurants, bars, home kitchens, family tables. **Never in wedding settings.** This is aligned with the enemy "Everyone leaves" — we photograph the afterward, not the day.

## B — IGNORED (NOT APPLICABLE)

Rules we don't fight because we don't touch them.

- Imagery of event rituals: confetti throws, bouquet tosses, sparklers, drone venue shots, champagne toasts, first dances, first kisses, first looks. We're not wedding photographers. We don't fight this territory.
- Wedding planner as authority. We don't sell to planners (future B2B2C possibility, not current audience).
- Bridal party as sub-clients. We don't market to bridesmaids individually.
- 2026 industry trends: AI wedding planning, weekend-long celebrations, hyper-personalization as marketing language. Our product intersects none of these as features.

---

## C — RESPECTED DELIBERATELY

We follow these rules on purpose. Documented so nobody future tries to "disrupt" them.

### C.1 — Warm earth-tone palette

Honey, Terracotta, Olive, Warm White, Sand, Soft Charcoal. We respect the category's warm palette because **the palette is not ownable in weddings**. Dozens of brands use warm neutrals. Breaking to blues, primary colors, or heavy dark punk aesthetics would generate confusion, not differentiation. We use the language of the category to be recognized as *in* the conversation, then differentiate elsewhere (narrative, prohibitions, vocabulary, positioning). *No future palette pivot should happen without founder approval.*

### C.2 — The book as rectangular bound object

We don't attempt gimmick formats (triangle books, rolled scrolls, deck of cards). The product's physical form obeys the expected format. Differentiation happens in cover design, typography, photography, and content — not in product shape.

## RISKS TO GUARD AGAINST

Rules that look like they apply to us and might pull the brand off-course if adopted unconsciously.

- **"Hyper-personalization" as marketing language.** Our product *is* personalized (each book is unique). But the industry's language of "hyper-customization" is now a cliché that every 2026 wedding brand uses. If we adopt that language, we sound generic. We sell **presence of 80 people**, not "personalized experience."
- **"Timeless" as aspiration.** The industry loves "timeless." It's banned vocabulary-adjacent. Our framing is not timeless — it's *active.* "Still at the table" is present tense, not eternal-past.
- **Light-and-airy photography of the product.** This is the wedding photobook industry default. Easy and cheap to produce. Seductive. We reject it.
