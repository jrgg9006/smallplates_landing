# Organizer — The Fourth Pillar

*Who we're talking to. Every piece of copy, every feature decision, every onboarding screen passes through her.*

*`foundation.md` defines her in a paragraph — enough to govern what the product is. This doc is the operational definition: how she thinks, what she fears, and how we write for her.*

---

## Who she is

She is a character, not a demographic.

She can be MOH, mom, sister, aunt, best friend — the label is irrelevant. What matters is the behavior: she's the one who opens the group chat, sends the reminders, follows up. Proactivity correlates with product success in production. Role does not.

She's the one who already got drafted, whether she asked for it or not. Small Plates exists because of her. Without her, the book doesn't start.

---

## What she carries

Three fears, always running in the background:

- **Ineptitude:** *"What if I can't pull this off?"*
- **Subjugation:** *"What if I do all the work and someone else gets the credit — or worse, the bride never knows?"*
- **Depletion:** *"What if this drains me when I'm already running on fumes?"*

Every screen she touches, every email she reads from us — if it raises any of these three, we lost her.

---

## Her secret question

> *"Can I pull this off without losing my mind — and still make her cry?"*

If the product answers yes to both halves, she buys. One half isn't enough.

---

## What she wants

Two things. Both should be visible in how we show up:

- **Glory without grief.**
- **A done-for-you system that makes her look like a genius.**

Not being rescued. Being backed. She's not overwhelmed; she's under-resourced. The distinction matters — it's the difference between a product that pities her and one that respects her.

---

## What trust looks like for her

For the bride, trust is built with warmth. For the organizer, trust is built with competence.

She doesn't need reassurance. She needs evidence. A dashboard that updates. Automated reminders that actually go out. Progress she can verify without asking. Organizer trust is operational: *"They have this handled. I don't need to worry."*

Everything we ship to her is evaluated on one axis: *does it reduce her cognitive load, or add to it?*

If it sounds like marketing, we failed. If it sounds like care, we succeeded.

---

## How we talk to her

The objections she'll raise, and how we answer them:

| She says | We say |
|---|---|
| *"This seems complicated."* | *"You invite. They send. We make the book. That's it. No design skills, no chasing people — we send the reminders."* |
| *"What if this becomes another thing I have to chase?"* | *"You don't chase anyone. Reminders are automated. You check the dashboard when you want — or don't. We flag anything that actually needs your attention."* |
| *"I don't want to be the nag."* | *"You won't be. We send the reminders — not you. Your name never appears on a follow-up. As far as the guests know, it's all coming from us."* |
| *"What if the bride thinks it's cheesy?"* | *"It won't be. Hardcover. Professionally designed. Minimal. If anything, she'll want to know where you found it."* |

Each answer does the same two things: removes the specific thing she's afraid of, and shows that we already thought about it. She doesn't want to be the first person to ever organize this. She wants to be the first in her group.

---

## Copy toolkit

The canonical lines for organizer-facing copy. Use these; don't invent new ones.

**Headlines and hooks:**
- *"A wedding gift made by everyone. Managed by no one."*
- *"Meaningful gift. Zero management overhead."*
- *"Bride cries. You're the hero. Nobody got stressed."*
- *"We send the reminders. You get the glory."*
- *"5 minutes. One recipe. You're in the book forever."* (for guest-facing moments inside the organizer flow)

**The Friendship Insurance angle** (for when the alternative is "I'll just do it myself"):

> *"DIY recipe books sound cute until you're texting your college roommate for the fourth time about a recipe she still hasn't sent. Small Plates removes that entirely. That's not a feature. That's insurance."*

This is the sharpest angle we have for organizer ads and landing variants. Use when the decision on the table is Small Plates vs. DIY.

---

## The dichotomy

The bride receives the gift. The organizer receives the glory. Both feel seen.
